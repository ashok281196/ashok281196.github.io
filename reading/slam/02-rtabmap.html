Chapter Two · Hands-on hardware

RTAB-Map with the Intel RealSense D435i

The cheapest credible SLAM rig on Earth: a $300 depth camera plus free ROS-native software. Almost everyone building indoor robots has run exactly this pair — which makes it perfect interview terrain.

2.1Why this exact pairing matters

Bolt a RealSense D435i onto anything with wheels, launch RTAB-Map, and out come a 2D navigation grid, a colored 3D point cloud, and an OctoMap — for the price of a mid-range phone. It's the standard indoor-UGV prototype rig, so it's a favorite interview topic: it tests whether you understand SLAM concepts and whether you've actually fought real hardware. This chapter gives you both.

2.2RTAB-Map: what it is, from zero

RTAB-Map — Real-Time Appearance-Based Mapping, by Mathieu Labbé & François Michaud (Université de Sherbrooke; IROS 2014, JFR 2019) — is a graph-based SLAM system. In the graph: nodes hold RGB-D keyframes (a color image + depth image + the odometry pose where they were taken); edges are odometry links, loop closures, and proximity links. Two ideas are its signature:

Signature 1 — appearance-based loop closure

"Appearance-based" means: recognize places by how they look, not by geometry. The mechanism, step by step:

  1. Extract local visual features from the current image (corners/blobs with descriptors — ORB, GFTT+BRIEF by default).
  2. Quantize them against a bag-of-visual-words (BoW) vocabulary — think of a dictionary of "visual words" (typical corner patterns); the image becomes a histogram of which words appear, like describing a book by its word counts.
  3. Compare that histogram against every stored location; a Bayesian filter maintains the probability that the current view matches each past place (probabilities accumulate over consecutive frames, so one lucky match isn't enough).
  4. The winning candidate is then geometrically verified: match individual features, solve the camera pose with PnP + RANSAC, and accept only if enough features agree (the Vis/MinInliers threshold, default ≈ 20).
  5. Only then is a loop-closure edge added and the graph re-optimized (g2o / GTSAM / Ceres).
PnP (Perspective-n-Point)
Given n known 3D points and where they appear in an image, solve for the camera pose. The workhorse of visual relocalization.
RANSAC
"Random Sample Consensus" — fit a model to random small subsets of the data, keep the fit that the most points agree with. The standard armor against wrong feature matches (outliers).
Keyframe
A selected frame kept for the long term (storing all 30 fps forever is pointless — consecutive frames are nearly identical).

Signature 2 — memory management (why it stays real-time forever)

A naive system compares each new frame against all past locations — cost grows with map size until real-time dies. RTAB-Map's fix is human-shaped: a Working Memory (WM) of recently/frequently seen locations that is actively searched, and a Long-Term Memory (LTM) on disk for the rest. Places not seen for a while are transferred out of WM; if a loop closure lands near an LTM area, its neighbors are pulled back in. Loop-closure search cost stays roughly constant regardless of map size → multi-session, building-scale mapping in real time on a CPU. This is the answer to "why does RTAB-Map scale?"

2.3The full architecture on a robot

D435i: RGB + depth (active IR stereo) + IMU (BMI055)
   ├─► odometry source (choose one):
   │     • rtabmap visual odometry: F2M (frame-to-local-map, default) or F2F
   │     • wheel odometry / external VIO, fused via robot_localization EKF
   ├─► rtabmap node: keyframes ─ graph ─ BoW loop closure ─ optimization
   └─► outputs: /map 2D grid · 3D point cloud · OctoMap · TF map→odom

Notes a practitioner would add: F2M (features-to-local-map) beats F2F (frame-to-frame) for the same scan-to-map-beats-scan-to-scan reason as Chapter 1. Reg/Strategy chooses how constraints are computed — visual (PnP on features), ICP (needs decent clouds), or visual-then-ICP-refine. Proximity detection links nodes that are near each other in space even when appearance matching fails — e.g., re-traversing a corridor in the opposite direction, where everything looks different but you're demonstrably in the same place.

2.4The D435i itself — hardware facts to get exactly right

  • Depth principle: active IR stereo. Two infrared cameras compute depth by stereo triangulation (like your two eyes), plus an IR dot projector that sprays invisible texture onto blank surfaces so stereo has something to match. Because the fallback is plain passive stereo, it still works outdoors in sunlight (noisier). It is not time-of-flight and not coded structured light (that was the older SR300) — interviewers check this.
  • Shutters: depth/IR sensors are global shutter (whole frame captured at one instant — motion-friendly); the RGB sensor is rolling shutter (rows captured sequentially → geometric wobble under fast motion). Consequence: feature tracking on RGB suffers during fast motion; a known trick is to run odometry on the left IR image instead — global shutter, and the projector's dots give free texture (dots are invisible to RGB but visible in IR).
  • The "i" = IMU: a 6-DOF BMI055. It's mediocre — fine for gravity direction and short-term motion prediction (via a Madgwick/complementary filter), not good enough to be your only odometry. It needs calibration (rs-imu-calibration for bias/scale) and correct camera-IMU extrinsics.
  • Envelope: depth is good roughly 0.3–3 m and degrades quadratically with distance (a stereo-baseline fact: farther = tinier disparity to measure). FOV ≈ 87°×58°.
  • Knobs: emitter power, presets (High Accuracy), post-processing filters (spatial/temporal/decimation) — but filters add latency and can smear geometry that ICP then trusts. Nothing is free.
  • The upgrade answer: "if I chose today, D455/D456" — 95 mm stereo baseline vs 50 mm (better depth at range), better IMU and sync.

2.5Field-tested gotchas (saying these = you actually shipped one)

1. Featureless white walls → visual odometry lost. No texture, no features, no motion estimate. Mitigations: run odometry on the IR image (the projector's dot pattern is free texture), or fuse wheel odometry so visual dropout is non-fatal.

2. Multiple D435i in one room: the IR projectors interfere mildly. Stereo tolerates alien dots far better than coded-light would (the dots are just texture, not a code), but for clean results stagger cameras or use hardware sync.

3. Direct sunlight: washes out the projector; depth falls back to passive stereo — works, noisier.

4. TF / time-sync bugs: wrong camera-to-robot extrinsics or unsynchronized timestamps produce the classic symptom — the map doubles or ghosts when the robot rotates. If you see double walls after a turn, check your transforms and clocks before blaming the algorithm.

5. The IMU needs calibrating — skip rs-imu-calibration and your gravity vector is subtly wrong forever.

2.6A few parameters worth knowing by name

Drop two or three of these in conversation — no more, or it sounds recited: Mem/STMSize and Rtabmap/TimeThr (the memory-management budget that keeps WM bounded), Vis/MinInliers (the loop-closure acceptance bar), Grid/RangeMax + Grid/RayTracing (how the 2D grid is generated from depth), Odom/ResetCountdown (auto-recover after lost odometry).

2.7Localization-only mode — the deployment split

Set Mem/IncrementalMemory=false and RTAB-Map stops adding to the map and instead localizes against the stored graph — the same BoW machinery, now used for relocalization. This is the standard product lifecycle: map once, localize daily. Chapter 5 generalizes this idea across every SLAM flavor.

2.9Interview questions — answer out loud first

Why does RTAB-Map stay real-time as the map grows to building scale?

Memory management. Loop-closure search runs only over the bounded Working Memory; older, unseen locations are transferred to Long-Term Memory and retrieved back only when a nearby loop closure makes them relevant. Search cost is therefore O(WM size) — constant — instead of O(map size). That's the design point of the whole system.

BoW loop closure in a building with identical-looking rooms — what saves you from a false match?

Three layers of defense: (1) the Bayesian filter requires evidence to accumulate over consecutive frames, not one lucky histogram match; (2) geometric verification — PnP + RANSAC must find a consistent pose with ≥ Vis/MinInliers agreeing features (identical-looking rooms rarely agree in precise 3D layout); (3) if a false closure still slips through, the graph becomes internally inconsistent — robust back-ends detect this with robust cost kernels or switchable constraints that let the optimizer "switch off" an edge that fights everything else.

D435i vs a lidar for a warehouse robot?

Refuse the either/or. The camera is cheap, gives dense depth plus semantics (labels, barcodes, humans) but has short range, narrow FOV, and lighting sensitivity. A 2D lidar gives 360°, long-range, lighting-immune geometry, and — decisive in a warehouse — can be part of a safety-rated stop system, which a RealSense cannot. Production answer: both. Lidar for robust localization + certified safety; camera for perception and cheap 3D obstacle sensing.

How do you get a 2D navigation map out of a 3D depth camera?

Take depth points within a height band (roughly floor-to-robot-height — obstacles that actually matter), project them onto the ground plane into grid cells as "occupied," then raytrace from the camera through free space to mark traversed cells "free" (RTAB-Map's Grid/* parameters, e.g. Grid/RangeMax, Grid/RayTracing). You've reduced 3D perception to exactly the occupancy grid Chapter 5's planners consume.

Visual odometry gets lost mid-run — what does the system do?

Layered recovery: Odom/ResetCountdown lets odometry reset itself after N lost frames; relocalization via the BoW machinery re-attaches the new trajectory to the known map at the next recognized place; and best practice is fusing wheel odometry (robot_localization EKF) so a visual dropout degrades accuracy instead of killing the run. The theme to voice: single-sensor odometry is a single point of failure; design for graceful degradation.
