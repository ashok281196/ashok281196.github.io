Before You StartHow to Read This Book

Read Chapter 0 first, even if it feels basic. Everything else in the book is built on the six ideas in that chapter: odometry, drift, loop closure, the map, optimization, and coordinate frames. Once those click, the fancy stuff (factor graphs, Gaussian splatting, differentiable bundle adjustment) turns out to be variations on the same six ideas wearing different costumes.

After that the book widens out. Chapters 1 and 2 give you the math and the hardware that every later chapter leans on. Chapters 3 through 7 cover the systems themselves: lidar, cameras, the budget RGB-D rig, the ORB-SLAM lineage in depth, and learned methods. Chapter 8 teaches you to measure any of it honestly, and Chapter 9 puts the map to work driving a robot.

Each chapter follows the same rhythm: intuition first, then the real machinery, then the systems people actually run, then a worked setup you can reproduce on your own hardware, a linked reading list, and finally interview questions with answers hidden behind click-to-reveal panels. Try answering out loud before you open them.

SLAM is one problem asked two ways at once: "Where am I?" (localization) and "What does the world look like?" (mapping). The cruel joke is that each answer requires the other. That circularity is the entire field.
