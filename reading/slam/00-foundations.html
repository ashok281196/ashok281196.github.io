Chapter Zero · Read this first

Foundations: the six ideas everything else is built on

Before a single algorithm name, let's make sure the problem itself is crystal clear — because SLAM is a weird, circular problem, and once you see the circle, the whole field makes sense.

0.1The problem, stated like a human

Imagine you wake up in an unfamiliar building with a notepad. Your job: draw a floor plan of the building and mark, at every moment, exactly where you are standing on that floor plan.

Notice the trap. To draw the walls in the right place on your map, you need to know where you are when you see them. But to know where you are, you need... a map to locate yourself against. Each answer depends on the other. This chicken-and-egg is the whole problem, and it has a name:

SLAM = Simultaneous Localization And Mapping. Build the map and track your own position in it — at the same time, from the same noisy sensor data, while moving.

Why does a robot need this at all? Because GPS doesn't work indoors, under bridges, in warehouses, in tunnels, or precisely enough for a robot that must pass through a 90 cm doorway. A robot with no SLAM is a robot that only knows "I told my wheels to spin" — it has no idea what actually happened.

0.2Idea 1 — Odometry: counting your steps

The simplest way to track yourself: start at a known point and add up your own motion. Wheels turned 10 times × wheel circumference = you moved 3.1 m forward. This is odometry (from the Greek hodos, "path"), also called dead reckoning.

Close your eyes and walk across a room, counting steps. Twenty steps forward, turn roughly 90°, ten steps more. You have an estimate of where you are. It's not bad! But every step was slightly uneven, your turn wasn't exactly 90°, and those small errors added up. Open your eyes after five minutes of this and you'll be surprised how far off you are.

That accumulation of error is called drift, and it is the villain of this entire book. Odometry error never averages out — it compounds. 1% error per meter sounds great until you've driven 500 meters and you're 5 meters off, which is the difference between "docked at the charger" and "in the pond."

Odometry
Estimating your motion incrementally from your own sensors — wheel encoders, an IMU, camera frames, or lidar scans compared to the previous one.
IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)
A chip with accelerometers + gyroscopes. Measures acceleration and rotation rate hundreds of times per second. Great for a fraction of a second; drifts terribly if you integrate it alone for long (errors get integrated twice to get position).
Drift
Accumulated error in an incremental estimate. Small per step, unbounded over time.
Pose
Position + orientation together. In 2D: (x, y, heading θ). In 3D: (x, y, z) plus 3 rotation angles — "6 degrees of freedom" (6-DOF).

0.3Idea 2 — Loop closure: recognizing where you've been

Here's the escape hatch from drift. Suppose, while blindfold-walking, you occasionally reach out and touch a landmark — "ah, this is the same doorframe I touched five minutes ago." The instant you recognize a previously-visited place, you learn something powerful: however much your step-counting says you've wandered, you are actually back here. You can now go back and correct your whole path — smear the accumulated error across every step you took in between.

That recognition event is called a loop closure, and the correction it triggers is what upgrades mere odometry into SLAM.

Odometry = local, smooth, drifts forever. Loop closure = occasional global truth injections. SLAM = odometry + loop closures + an optimizer that reconciles them. Every system in this book, no matter how fancy, is this sentence.

0.4Idea 3 — The map: what are we actually drawing?

"Map" can mean several very different data structures, and choosing one is a real engineering decision:

Map typeWhat it storesGood for
Landmark / feature mapA sparse cloud of distinctive points ("corner of the poster at x,y,z")Localization — cheap to match against
Occupancy gridThe world chopped into cells, each labeled occupied / free / unknownPlanning safe paths — "can I drive through this cell?"
Point cloudRaw 3D points from lidar/depth sensors, stitched togetherVisualization, registration, surveying

Keep the word "unknown" in mind for occupancy grids — a good map distinguishes "I know this is empty" from "I've never looked there." An exploring robot lives on that difference.

0.5Idea 4 — Everything is uncertain, so everything is probability

Every sensor lies a little. Wheels slip. Lidar ranges have noise. Cameras blur. So SLAM never says "I am at (3.20, 5.10)"; it says "I believe I'm near (3.20, 5.10), give or take this much" — a probability distribution over poses. The math machinery of the field is about maintaining and updating that belief:

  • Predict: use your motion (odometry) to shift the belief forward — and widen it, because motion adds uncertainty.
  • Correct: use a sensor observation ("the wall is 2.1 m ahead") to narrow the belief — observations remove uncertainty.

This predict/correct heartbeat is the Bayes filter, and its two famous children are the Kalman filter (belief is a single Gaussian blob — fast, but can only believe in one place at a time) and the particle filter (belief is a swarm of thousands of guesses — can believe in several places at once, which matters in Chapter 5's "kidnapped robot" problem). The bible for all of this is Probabilistic Robotics — Thrun, Burgard & Fox (MIT Press, 2005).

Kalman filter = one confident friend pointing at the map saying "we're here-ish." Particle filter = a crowd of 5,000 tourists each dropping a pin; the pins that match what you see out the window survive, the rest get deleted and respawned near the survivors.

0.6Idea 5 — Modern SLAM is an optimization problem (the graph)

Filters update a belief step by step and throw the past away. Modern SLAM keeps the past and solves it all at once. Picture a graph:

  • Nodes = your pose at each moment ("where I was at t=1, t=2, ...").
  • Edges = measurements connecting poses: "odometry says node 5 is 1 m ahead of node 4," "loop closure says node 200 is the same place as node 12."

Each edge is a soft constraint — a spring. Odometry springs chain the nodes together; a loop closure adds a long-range spring from late in the trajectory back to early. Then you relax the spring network: find the set of poses that stretches all springs the least. That "least total squared stretch" is least-squares optimization, and this formulation is called pose graph optimization — or in its general form, a factor graph. Libraries that solve it: GTSAM, Ceres Solver, g2o.

When a loop closure "snaps the map into place," what physically happened is: one new spring was added to the graph, and the solver re-relaxed the whole network. Remember the springs — you'll reuse this picture in every chapter.
Front-end
The part that processes raw sensor data into constraints: feature matching, scan matching, loop closure detection. Deals with pixels and points.
Back-end
The optimizer that takes those constraints and solves for the best poses/map. Deals with math. "Front-end proposes, back-end disposes."
Bundle adjustment (BA)
The camera version of graph optimization: jointly refine camera poses and 3D point positions so that points re-project onto the images where they were actually seen. The gold standard of accuracy in visual SLAM.

0.7Idea 6 — Coordinate frames: the robot's family tree

A robot juggles several coordinate systems at once, and confusing them causes the most embarrassing bugs in robotics. The standard chain (ROS convention, REP 105):

map ──► odom ──► base_link ──► (sensor frames: camera, lidar, imu...)
  • base_link — the robot's own body. "Forward" for the robot.
  • odom — where odometry thinks the robot has moved since power-on. Smooth and continuous, but drifts.
  • map — the globally-correct world frame. The SLAM system publishes the correction between map and odom. This correction can jump when a loop closure fires.

Why this weird split? Because the motor controller must never see the robot's position teleport 30 cm mid-motion. Smooth things (steering) live in odom; correct things (global plans) live in map. Chapter 5 turns this into a superpower — it's the single most senior-sounding thing you can say in an interview.

SensorGives youLovesHates

No sensor is sufficient alone — which is why the word fusion appears in every system name (LIO = Lidar-Inertial Odometry, VIO = Visual-Inertial Odometry). Sensors are chosen so that one's weakness is another's strength: IMU carries you through the 0.1 s the camera is blurred; the camera cancels the IMU's drift.

0.10Check yourself (interview warm-ups)

Explain SLAM to a non-engineer in three sentences.

A robot in a new building must draw the floor plan while also tracking its own position on that plan — but drawing needs your position and finding your position needs the plan, so both must be estimated together. The robot counts its own motion (which slowly drifts) and corrects itself whenever it recognizes a place it has seen before. Software fuses all of these noisy clues into the single most consistent map-plus-trajectory.

Why does odometry drift, and why can't better sensors fully fix it?

Each incremental motion estimate carries a small error, and position is the sum of all increments — so errors accumulate rather than cancel. Better sensors shrink the per-step error but the sum is still unbounded over time. Only absolute information — recognizing a known place (loop closure), a known map, or GPS — can bound it, because it constrains where you are rather than how you moved.

What's the difference between a filter-based and a graph-based SLAM back-end?

A filter (Kalman/particle) maintains only the current belief and marginalizes the past — cheap, online, but a mistake baked in stays in. A graph keeps (a subset of) all past poses as variables and re-optimizes them whenever new constraints arrive — so a loop closure can retroactively fix the whole trajectory. Modern systems are graph-based, often with a filter (or fixed-lag smoother) running the fast odometry front-end.

Why do robots separate the map frame from the odom frame?

Odometry is smooth but drifting; SLAM corrections are accurate but arrive as discrete jumps. Controllers steering the wheels must see a smooth pose (odom→base_link), while planners need the globally correct one (map). Publishing the correction as a separate map→odom transform lets a loop-closure jump update plans without teleporting the robot under its controller — full story in Chapter 5.
