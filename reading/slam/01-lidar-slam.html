Chapter One · Lasers

LiDAR SLAM & the mapping pipeline

One skeleton diagram, held firmly in your head, explains every laser-based SLAM system built since 2014 — from LOAM to FAST-LIO2, and from OctoMap to the distance fields that replaced it.

1.1First: what a lidar actually gives you

A lidar fires laser pulses and times how long each takes to bounce back — light travel time → distance, to centimeter accuracy. A spinning 3D lidar does this hundreds of thousands of times per second across many vertical beams, so ten times a second you receive a scan: a cloud of ~100,000 3D points showing the geometry around you, in the sensor's own coordinates. It works in pitch darkness and doesn't care about texture. What it gives you is shape; what it can't give you is color, meaning, or — crucially — where you are. That last part is our job.

1.2The skeleton (memorize this one diagram)

raw scan ─► deskew (motion compensation, IMU)
        ─► odometry (scan-to-map matching)
        ─► local map (submap / ikd-tree / voxel grid)
        ─► loop closure (place recognition + geometric verify)
        ─► global optimization (pose graph / factor graph)
        ─► map products: occupancy · TSDF/ESDF · mesh
Every LiDAR SLAM system ever built is a variation on these six boxes. When an interviewer says "how does lidar SLAM work?" — you walk this diagram, left to right, one sentence per box. Let's now earn the right to do that by understanding each box.

1.3Box 1 — Deskew: the rolling-shutter problem of lasers

A spinning lidar takes ~100 ms to complete one revolution. If the robot is moving at 2 m/s, the last point of the scan was captured from a position 20 cm away from where the first was. Treat the scan as one rigid snapshot and your walls come out smeared and curved.

Deskew (motion compensation) fixes this: estimate the motion during the sweep — usually by integrating the IMU, or assuming constant velocity — and re-project every point to where it would be if the whole scan were taken at a single instant. Skip this step and everything downstream degrades the moment you move fast.

It's a panorama photo taken while leaning out of a moving train: unless you account for the train's motion between the first and last frame, your stitched panorama bends. Deskew is un-bending it.

1.4Box 2 — Scan matching: the engine of lidar odometry

Given the new scan and the map (or previous scan), find the rotation + translation that lines them up best. That alignment is your motion estimate. Three classic algorithms:

  • ICP — Iterative Closest Point. Repeat: (1) for each point in the new scan, find its nearest neighbor in the map; (2) compute the transform that minimizes distance between these pairs; (3) apply, repeat until it stops improving. The point-to-plane variant minimizes distance to the local surface instead of the point itself — converges much faster on real structured scenes. GICP generalizes both by treating each point as a small Gaussian.
  • NDT — Normal Distributions Transform. Chop the map into voxels, fit a Gaussian to the points in each voxel, then align the new scan to maximize its likelihood under those Gaussians. Smoother cost surface than ICP; popular in autonomous driving stacks.

Failure modes — this is where interviews go:

Bad initial guess → wrong local minimum. ICP is a hill climber. Start it too far from the truth (fast rotation, low scan rate) and it happily locks onto the wrong alignment. Cure: better motion prediction (IMU), coarse-to-fine matching.

Degenerate geometry → unconstrained directions. In a long featureless corridor, every position along the corridor produces an identical scan — the math literally cannot observe that axis. Same for a flat open field (yaw + translation poorly constrained). The estimate slides freely along the degenerate direction. Detection: the optimization's Hessian/covariance has a near-zero eigenvalue along that axis. Cure: fuse a sensor that does observe it — wheel odometry, IMU — and down-weight the lidar along the sick direction.

Note the vocabulary upgrade: matching each scan to the previous scan ("scan-to-scan") accumulates error fast; matching to an accumulated local map ("scan-to-map") is what modern systems do — the map averages away individual-scan noise.

1.5Boxes 4–5 — Loop closure & the factor graph

Odometry alone drifts (Chapter 0). So we add two things:

Place recognition for point clouds. How do you recognize "I've been here" from a bag of 3D points? The classic answer is Scan Context (Kim & Kim, IROS 2018): flatten the scan into a polar image (rings × sectors of max height) — a fingerprint you can compare cheaply against every past fingerprint. Successors: Scan Context++, STD, BTC; learned ones (OverlapTransformer, LoGG3D-Net) appear in Chapter 4. Any candidate match is then geometrically verified (run ICP; accept only if it converges tightly) — because a false loop closure is poison: it welds two different places together and bends the whole map.

The factor graph back-end. Recall the spring network from Chapter 0: poses are nodes; odometry edges, loop-closure edges, and IMU constraints are factors (springs). Solved with GTSAM or Ceres using Gauss-Newton / Levenberg-Marquardt. When a loop closure fires and the map visibly snaps, what you're watching is this graph relaxing.

1.6The classic systems (still asked about by name)

SystemThe one ideaStatus
LOAM (2014)Split odometry into two speeds: a fast, rough scan-to-scan matcher on extracted edge and plane features, plus a slow, precise scan-to-map refiner. Fast loop keeps you real-time; slow loop keeps you accurate.The ancestor of everything
LeGO-LOAM (2018)LOAM made light for ground robots: segment the ground plane first, optimize the ground-related and wall-related parts of the pose separately.Legacy
Cartographer (Google, 2016)Build small local submaps (probability grids); match scans to the current submap (Ceres). For loop closure, search all past submaps using branch-and-bound — a clever tree search over a multi-resolution stack of the grid that prunes hopeless regions wholesale, making a brute-force global search real-time. Sparse pose graph ties submaps together.Maintained-ish; still excellent for 2D indoor
LIO-SAM (2020)Tightly-coupled lidar + IMU as one factor graph (GTSAM), using IMU preintegration factors — hundreds of IMU readings compressed into a single constraint between consecutive lidar poses.Widely deployed

Cartographer's branch-and-bound loop search is genuinely elegant — if an interviewer asks "tell me about an algorithm you admire," it is a strong, slightly-unusual pick: exact global search made real-time by bounding, no learning required.

Tightly vs loosely coupled
Loosely coupled: each sensor produces its own pose estimate, and you blend the outputs (e.g., in an EKF). Tightly coupled: raw measurements from all sensors enter one joint optimization. Tighter = more accurate, especially when one sensor is briefly weak, at the cost of complexity.
IMU preintegration
Trick (Forster et al.) letting you summarize all IMU samples between two poses into one factor, so re-optimizing the graph doesn't require re-integrating raw IMU data. Enables IMU use in graph back-ends.

1.7The modern era (say these to sound current)

  • FAST-LIO2 (Xu et al., 2021) — the de-facto default lidar-inertial odometry today. Two departures: (1) no feature extraction at all — register raw points directly to the map (feature extraction throws away information and breaks on unusual lidars); (2) the map lives in an ikd-tree — an incremental k-d tree that supports fast insertion/deletion, so the map structure updates in milliseconds instead of being rebuilt. Fusion is a tightly-coupled iterated extended Kalman filter with the IMU.
  • Point-LIO (He et al., 2023) — updates the filter per point rather than per scan. No scan accumulation delay → survives violently aggressive motion (spinning drones), outputs odometry at kHz rates.
  • KISS-ICP (Vizzo et al., 2023) — the beautiful counter-punch: plain point-to-point ICP + a constant-velocity motion model + one adaptive threshold. No IMU, essentially no parameters, shockingly robust across cars, drones, and handhelds. The perfect interview answer for "what baseline do you try first?"
  • DLIO (2023) — direct lidar-inertial with continuous-time motion correction (deskew and integration treated as one smooth trajectory).
  • GLIM and MOLA — modern modular back-end frameworks, useful to name when asked about extensible architectures.

1.8Box 6 — Map products: OctoMap and what replaced it

SLAM's pose output is only half the deliverable; the other half is a map another module can use. Two big families:

Occupancy: "is this cell blocked, free, or unknown?"

OctoMap (Hornung et al., 2013) is the classic: an octree (a 3D tree where each cube splits into 8 children) storing per-cell occupancy as log-odds — a running score nudged up on a laser hit, nudged down for every cell a beam passed through (found by raycasting along the beam), and clamped so cells can change their mind later. It distinguishes free from unknown space, which planners and explorers need. Its weakness after a decade: pointer-chasing through the tree is slow at scale.

Bonxai (Faconti, 2023) is the "modern OctoMap" — a VDB-style hierarchical voxel grid (the structure from movie-VFX volumetrics) with no per-node pointers, cache-friendly, drop-in faster. wavemap (ETH, 2023) compresses occupancy with wavelets for accuracy + compactness.

Distance fields: "how far is the nearest surface?"

TSDF (truncated signed distance field) stores, near surfaces, the signed distance to the surface (negative inside, positive outside) — the representation depth-camera reconstruction is built on. Integrate it outward and you get an ESDF (Euclidean SDF): every cell knows its distance to the nearest obstacle, and the gradient of that field points away from trouble — exactly what trajectory optimizers want. The reference pipeline is Voxblox (Oleynikova et al., 2016); its GPU-accelerated descendant nvblox (NVIDIA) runs real-time on a Jetson — worth name-dropping if the target platform is NVIDIA edge hardware.

The one-liner: occupancy answers "may I enter this cell?" (graph-search planners, exploration, sensor fusion via raycast); TSDF/ESDF answers "how far is the nearest surface, and in which direction?" (gradient-based trajectory optimization). Planners often want the ESDF; the ESDF is built from a TSDF or an occupancy map.

And the research frontier: PIN-SLAM (2024) and SHINE-Mapping make the map a small neural network (an implicit SDF) — bridge to Chapter 4.

1.10Interview questions — answer out loud first

Lidar odometry drifts along the axis of a long corridor — why, and what do you do about it?

Why: geometric degeneracy. Both walls are parallel planes; sliding the scan along the corridor axis changes nothing about how well it matches. That direction is unobservable to scan matching, so the estimate wanders freely along it while staying perfect in the other axes.

Detect: look at the eigenvalues of the optimization's information matrix (or covariance) — a near-zero eigenvalue flags the sick direction, online.

Fix: fuse a sensor that does observe that axis — wheel odometry or IMU-derived velocity — and constrain or down-weight the lidar solution along the degenerate eigenvector. Longer term: loop closure at the corridor's end snaps accumulated error back.

How does OctoMap update a cell from a laser scan?

For each beam, raycast from the sensor to the hit point. Every cell the ray passes through gets its log-odds occupancy decremented (evidence of free space); the endpoint cell gets incremented (evidence of a surface). Log-odds are clamped to min/max so a cell never becomes so certain it can't change later — vital in dynamic environments. Cells never touched by any ray stay "unknown," which is a distinct state from "free."

A loop closure fires mid-mission and the map jumps 30 cm. What happens to navigation?

The pose graph relaxed and the map→odom correction jumped — but the odom→base_link chain the local controller consumes stayed smooth, so the wheels never see a teleport. The global planner, working in the map frame, replans on the corrected map. This frame-contract answer is developed fully in Chapter 5 — it's the expected senior-level response.

When would you choose Cartographer over FAST-LIO2?

Cartographer: 2D indoor mapping, no or poor IMU, and you want its built-in occupancy grid output plus a pure-localization mode for deployment — a complete indoor package. FAST-LIO2: 3D, aggressive motion, good IMU available, and you need the fastest most robust odometry — but you assemble your own loop closure and map products around it. Different jobs: complete 2D system vs best-in-class 3D odometry engine.

Design a mapping stack for a robot you know well — which pieces from this chapter and why?

Structure the answer as the skeleton: choose an odometry engine (FAST-LIO2 if IMU + 3D lidar; KISS-ICP as the simple baseline first), add Scan Context + ICP verification for loop closure, GTSAM pose graph on top, then generate two map products — an occupancy grid (Bonxai/OctoMap) for planning and coverage, and optionally a TSDF/ESDF (nvblox if there's a Jetson) for smooth trajectory optimization. Mention streaming the cloud to your visualization/dashboard layer — connecting the pipeline to a real product you've built is what makes the answer land.
