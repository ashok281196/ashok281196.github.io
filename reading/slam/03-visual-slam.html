Chapter Three · Cameras, six eras

Visual SLAM — the state of the art, early 2026

Ten years ago the answer was "ORB-SLAM." Today the answer is a taxonomy. Learn the six eras and you can place any paper, product, or interview question on the map in one sentence.

3.1Why cameras are the hard mode

A lidar hands you 3D geometry directly. A camera hands you a grid of brightness values — geometry must be inferred by watching how pixels move between frames. Worse, a single camera fundamentally cannot tell a small room nearby from a big room far away: everything is known only up to scale (the famous monocular scale ambiguity). In exchange, cameras are ten times cheaper, tiny, power-sipping, and see color and meaning. The history of visual SLAM is the history of squeezing geometry out of pixels with progressively more machine learning.

3.3Eras 1–2 — the baselines production robots still run

ORB-SLAM3 (Campos et al., 2021) is the reference feature-based system: mono/stereo/RGB-D, tight IMU fusion, and its signature Atlas — when tracking is lost, instead of dying it spawns a fresh map and merges it with the old one when a place is re-recognized (via the DBoW2 bag-of-words). Still the accuracy champion on the standard benchmarks. Weaknesses: hand-crafted ORB features die in motion blur and low texture, and the output map is a sparse point cloud — great for localizing, useless for navigating directly.

Direct methods (DSO — Engel et al., 2016) ask: why extract features at all? Align images by minimizing the brightness difference of pixels directly. More information used, beautiful dense-ish maps — but now you're assuming brightness is consistent, so exposure changes, rolling shutter, and uncalibrated photometrics hurt badly.

Reprojection error
"I triangulated this 3D point; when I project it back into the image, how far is it from where I actually detected it?" Era 1 minimizes this.
Photometric error
"If I warp frame A onto frame B using my pose+depth estimate, how different are the pixel brightnesses?" Era 2 minimizes this.
VIO (Visual-Inertial Odometry)
Camera + IMU fused, odometry only (no loop closure/mapping). If the question is "drone state estimation," the answer is VIO, not full SLAM: VINS-Fusion, OpenVINS, OKVIS2 are the workhorses.

3.4Era 3 — learned front-ends: the pragmatic 2024–26 production answer

Observation: classical geometry back-ends are near-optimal; what breaks in practice is appearance — detecting and matching features under blur, weak texture, day/night change. So replace exactly those parts with networks and keep the geometric guarantees:

  • SuperPoint (DeTone et al., 2018) — learned keypoint detector+descriptor, self-supervised.
  • LightGlue (Lindenberger et al., 2023) — fast learned matcher (the efficient successor of SuperGlue); adapts its compute to how hard the image pair is. LoFTR / RoMa go further: dense, detector-free matching.
  • Place recognition upgraded from BoW to learned global descriptors: NetVLAD (2016) → MixVPR, SALAD, MegaLoc (2025).
  • The standard toolbox gluing these into visual relocalization: hloc — hierarchical localization.
Era 3 is the "what would you actually ship in 2026" answer: learned front-end where appearance is brittle, classical optimization back-end where geometry is already optimal. Cheap to adopt, keeps interpretability. Chapter 4 dissects this philosophy.

3.5Era 4 — the DROID lineage

DROID-SLAM (Teed & Deng, NeurIPS 2021): a recurrent network (GRU) looks at pairs of frames and repeatedly revises a dense correspondence field; after each revision, a differentiable bundle adjustment (DBA) layer — an actual geometric least-squares solver, embedded inside the network with gradients flowing through it — updates all camera poses and per-pixel depths to be consistent with those correspondences. Predict → solve → repeat.

The result is eerily robust — it essentially never loses tracking where ORB-SLAM3 dies — but it wants a serious GPU and runs offline-ish. DPVO (Teed, Lipson & Deng, 2023) made it cheap: track sparse random patches instead of dense flow → real-time visual odometry on one modest GPU; DPV-SLAM (2024) added loop closure and global optimization back.

The positioning sentence to remember: "DROID showed that end-to-end learning plus a differentiable geometric solver beats classical robustness; DPVO made it affordable."

3.6Era 5 — neural-rendering SLAM: the map is a renderer

Radical inversion: make the map a differentiable rendering function. To track the camera, render what the map predicts you'd see from a guessed pose and optimize the pose (and the map) until the render matches the real image.

  • NeRF-based wave: iMAP (2021) → NICE-SLAM (2022) → Co-SLAM / ESLAM / Point-SLAM (2023). Bottleneck: NeRF rendering is slow, and SLAM needs to render constantly.
  • 3D Gaussian Splatting wave (2024): represent the scene as millions of soft 3D Gaussian blobs that rasterize fast — suddenly real-time photorealistic RGB-D SLAM: SplaTAM (2024), MonoGS — "Gaussian Splatting SLAM" (CVPR 2024), Photo-SLAM, RTG-SLAM, GS-ICP-SLAM (couples ICP with the splats).

Why care: the output is a dense, photorealistic map you can re-render from anywhere — perfect for inspection, telepresence, digital twins, and simulation.

The honest critique to voice out loud (interviewers respect it): benchmarks are mostly room-scale RGB-D; drift handling, loop closure, and metric scale outdoors still lag geometric systems; compute is heavy. As of early 2026 this is the reconstruction answer, not the navigation-stack answer.

3.7Era 6 — feed-forward geometry: the 2025–26 frontier

Every previous era solves for geometry per scene. Era 6 asks: what if a transformer, trained on millions of scenes, just predicts the geometry in one forward pass?

  • DUSt3R (Wang et al., CVPR 2024) — feed two images (uncalibrated! it doesn't even need to know the camera's intrinsics) into a transformer; out come dense pointmaps: a 3D point for every pixel, already expressed in a shared coordinate frame. No feature matching, no triangulation — the network has internalized geometry as a prior. MASt3R adds metric scale and matching.
  • MASt3R-SLAM (Murai et al., 2025) — real-time SLAM built on those priors: uncalibrated monocular video, in the wild.
  • VGGT (Wang et al., CVPR 2025) — one feed-forward transformer emits cameras, depths, and pointmaps for many views in seconds; VGGT-SLAM scales it with submap alignment. Streaming/causal variants: Spann3R, CUT3R, StreamVGGT; MegaSaM (2024–25) gets robust structure+motion from casual phone video.
  • Supporting cast: monocular metric depth foundation models — Depth Anything v2 (2024), Metric3D v2, UniDepth — dropped into classical pipelines as priors that dissolve the scale ambiguity.
The era-6 positioning line: "Geometry is becoming a learned prior that you query, not something you re-derive per scene. SLAM's remaining job becomes alignment + memory management on top of foundation-model outputs." Say this and you sound like you read papers last month.

3.8Benchmarks & metrics — the vocabulary layer

  • Datasets: EuRoC MAV (drone VIO), TUM RGB-D, KITTI (driving odometry), TartanAir (brutal synthetic), 7-Scenes / ScanNet (relocalization & reconstruction).
  • Metrics: ATE — absolute trajectory error after aligning the estimated and true trajectories (global consistency); RPE — relative pose error over short segments (drift rate). Reconstruction: chamfer distance; rendering-SLAM adds PSNR.

3.10Interview questions — answer out loud first

ORB-SLAM3 vs DROID-SLAM — when would you use each?

ORB-SLAM3: CPU/embedded budgets, mature multi-sensor support (stereo, RGB-D, IMU), best-in-class accuracy when features are trackable. DROID-SLAM: needs a big GPU but is dramatically more robust — it essentially never loses tracking. Accuracy is comparable; the failure modes differ: features die in blur and texture-poor scenes; DROID rarely dies but, run monocular, drifts in scale. Choose by compute budget and by which failure you can least afford.

Is 3D Gaussian Splatting SLAM ready for a product in 2026?

Split by product. For reconstruction-shaped products — inspection, telepresence, digital twins, sim assets — yes-ish: real-time photorealistic dense mapping is genuinely delivered. For the navigation stack — no: benchmarks are room-scale RGB-D, outdoor drift/loop-closure/scale still trail geometric systems, and compute competes with everything else on the robot. Giving the split answer, with the critique, is exactly what "senior" sounds like.

How do foundation models change SLAM?

Tell the DUSt3R story: a transformer trained on millions of scenes regresses dense 3D pointmaps from uncalibrated images in one forward pass — no per-scene feature matching or triangulation. Geometry becomes a prior you query. The SLAM layer's remaining job shrinks to fusing, aligning, and memory-managing those outputs (MASt3R-SLAM, VGGT-SLAM), plus metric-depth priors (Depth Anything v2) dissolving monocular scale ambiguity inside classical pipelines.

Monocular scale ambiguity — how is it solved across the eras?

One camera can't distinguish "small and near" from "large and far," so scale is unobservable. Fixes: add an IMU (accelerometer measures metric acceleration → scale becomes observable — the VIO answer), add a second camera (stereo baseline is a known metric ruler), or — the modern option — use a learned metric-depth prior (Depth Anything v2, Metric3D) trained on data where true scale was known.

Pick a visual SLAM approach for a teleoperated robot whose GPU also encodes video — justify.

Budget reasoning first: the GPU is already busy, so DROID-class and splatting SLAM are out. Answer: a hybrid — lightweight VIO (OpenVINS/VINS-Fusion) for smooth real-time state estimation, plus era-3 learned relocalization (SuperPoint + LightGlue via hloc) against a prebuilt map for drift correction. You keep robustness where it's cheap (front-end) and spend nothing on neural rendering you don't need. Interviewers reward the resource-constraint framing more than the name-drops.
