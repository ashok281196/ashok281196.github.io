Chapter Eight · Numbers or It Didn't Happen

Evaluation, Datasets & Benchmarks

Anyone can show you a map that looks right. This chapter is about proving it: the metrics, the ground truth, the datasets, and a protocol you can defend in a design review.

8.1Why Evaluation Is Its Own Skill

Every SLAM demo looks right. The point cloud is dense, the trajectory is a smooth ribbon, the loop visibly snaps shut in the viewer. And none of that tells you whether the robot ended the run 4 cm or 4 m from where it believed it was. Human eyes are spectacularly bad at judging metric accuracy from a 3D viewer: a map can be off by 2% of scale, sheared by a degree of uncalibrated IMU mounting, or missing an entire wing that the operator never drove through, and it will still screenshot beautifully.

The deeper problem is that in the field there is no ground truth. Your warehouse has no motion capture rig. So evaluation splits into two distinct activities: benchmarking on public datasets where someone spent real money on a reference trajectory, which tells you how your algorithm compares to the literature, and field evaluation on your own robot, which requires you to manufacture partial ground truth (surveyed markers, return-to-start tricks, a slower but better reference system) and to be honest about what you can and cannot claim. Both are engineering work, not an afterthought for the last week of the project.

It is also the interviewer's favorite trap. "Your SLAM works. Prove it." Candidates who answer with viewer screenshots stop being senior candidates at that moment. The expected answer names a metric, an alignment convention, a dataset, a number of runs, and a failure-counting policy. By the end of this chapter that answer will be reflex.

A SLAM system without an evaluation protocol is a demo. The protocol is: which metric, aligned how, against what reference of known accuracy, over how many runs, with failures counted how. Five decisions. Interviewers probe all five.

8.2Trajectory Metrics That Matter

Two metrics dominate, both introduced to the community in the form we use today by Sturm et al.'s TUM RGB-D benchmark (IROS 2012): absolute trajectory error and relative pose error. They answer different questions, and each one lies in a specific way, so you report both.

Align First, Then Measure

Before any comparison, remember that your estimator starts at its own arbitrary origin. The estimate lives in one coordinate frame, the ground truth in another, and the offset between them is not an error, it is a gauge freedom (Chapter 1's unobservable directions, back again). So step zero is to find the rigid transform that best fits the estimate onto the reference, in the least-squares sense. The closed-form solution is Umeyama's method (1991): given two matched point sets it returns the optimal rotation, translation, and optionally a scale factor. For metric systems (stereo, RGB-D, lidar, VIO) you align with a scale-free SE(3) transform. For monocular vision, where scale is unobservable, you must align in Sim(3), letting scale float; anything else measures the arbitrary initial scale rather than the algorithm.

That scale switch is not a technicality. A Sim(3)-aligned monocular result silently forgives all global scale error, so a mono number and a stereo number in the same table are not comparing the same thing. Papers that hide this in a footnote are committing sin number two of Section 8.8.

ATE and RPE, Precisely

Notation: Q_1..Q_N ground truth poses, P_1..P_N estimated poses, both in SE(3),
          matched by timestamp. trans(.) is the translation part, angle(.) the
          rotation angle of a transform.

Step 0 (alignment):  S = argmin over S of  SUM || trans(S * P_i) - trans(Q_i) ||^2
                     S in SE(3) for metric systems, Sim(3) for monocular.
                     Closed form: Umeyama 1991.

ATE:   F_i = Q_i^-1 * S * P_i                      (residual at pose i)
       ATE_rmse = sqrt( 1/N * SUM || trans(F_i) ||^2 )
       (also report mean and median; rotation ATE from angle(F_i) if wanted)

RPE:   over pose pairs (i, i+D), D fixed in seconds, meters, or frames:
       E_i = ( Q_i^-1 * Q_{i+D} )^-1 * ( P_i^-1 * P_{i+D} )
       RPE_trans = rmse of || trans(E_i) ||        RPE_rot = rmse of angle(rot(E_i))
       No global alignment needed: relative motions cancel the frame offset.

ATE measures global consistency: after the best rigid fit, how far is each estimated pose from where it should be? Loop closures improve it dramatically because they bend the whole trajectory back into shape. RPE measures local accuracy: over each 1-second or 10-meter chunk, how wrong was the estimated motion? It is the drift-rate metric, and it barely notices loop closures. The classic pattern: a loop-closing system posts a great ATE with mediocre RPE (globally consistent, locally jittery), while a good odometry-only stack posts excellent RPE and an ATE that grows with every meter of drift. If you feed a controller (Chapter 9), RPE jitter is what shakes the robot; if you draw a floor plan, ATE is what warps it.

ATE is grading a traced drawing: slide and rotate the tracing paper until it fits the original as well as possible, then measure the remaining gaps everywhere. RPE ignores where the paper sits and instead compares each individual pencil stroke, its length and its direction, to the corresponding stroke in the original. A drawing can have perfect strokes that accumulate into the wrong overall shape, and vice versa.

Drift as a Percentage, and Per Axis

For odometry, the most communicable numbers are normalized: translational drift as a percentage of distance traveled and rotational drift in degrees per meter. This is exactly what the KITTI odometry benchmark computes: RPE evaluated over sub-trajectories of 100 to 800 m, averaged, reported as % and deg/m. Calibrated expectations: a competitive stereo or lidar odometry sits below 1% translation drift, KITTI leaders below 0.5%, and rotation around 0.002 to 0.005 deg/m (roughly 2 to 5 degrees per kilometer). If your stack drifts 5% you have a bug or a calibration problem, not a tuning problem.

Since drift percentages only mean something against a baseline, calibrate your expectations with typical good numbers by system class:

System classA good result looks likeMeasured on

Also look at errors per axis before averaging them away. With an IMU in the loop, gravity makes roll and pitch observable, so real drift concentrates in yaw and translation (the four unobservable directions of VIO). A ballooning z error on a ground robot usually means bad extrinsics or a degenerate scene (Chapter 3's long corridors), and you will only see it if you plot x, y, z separately.

Timestamps, the Silent Killer

ATE and RPE both require matched pose pairs, and estimate and ground truth almost never share timestamps. Tools associate by nearest neighbor within a tolerance (evo's default --t_max_diff is 0.01 s) and can apply a constant --t_offset for unsynchronized clocks. Do the arithmetic on what sloppiness costs: at 1 m/s, a 100 ms clock offset injects 10 cm of pure fiction into ATE, which is larger than the true error of every serious system in this book. If your numbers look mysteriously bad, audit time first (Chapter 2's clock-sync material pays its rent here).

ATE / APE
Absolute trajectory error: per-pose residual against ground truth after a best global alignment. evo generalizes it as APE (absolute pose error), which can also score rotation or the full pose.
RPE
Relative pose error: mismatch of motion increments over a fixed step in time, distance, or frames. The drift metric. Alignment-free by construction.
Umeyama alignment
Closed-form least-squares fit of rotation, translation, and optional scale between two matched point sets. What --align runs under the hood.
Sim(3)
SE(3) plus a scale factor, 7 degrees of freedom. Required alignment group for monocular results; forgives global scale error by design.
Association
Matching estimated and reference poses by timestamp before comparison. Wrong tolerance or a clock offset corrupts every downstream statistic.
ATE scores the trajectory as a shape after the best rigid fit. RPE scores the motion increments and is immune to alignment. Report both, state the alignment group (SE(3) or Sim(3)), and normalize drift as % of distance and deg/m so numbers transfer between routes.

8.3The Tooling: evo and Friends

Nobody computes these by hand. The community standard is evo, a Python package that reads every common trajectory format, aligns, computes APE and RPE with full statistics, plots, and packages results into zip files you can compare across runs. The command vocabulary is small:

pip install evo --upgrade

evo_ape tum  gt.txt est.txt -va --plot --save_results run1_ape.zip   # -a align, -v verbose
evo_ape tum  gt.txt est_mono.txt -vas          # -s adds scale: Sim(3), monocular only
evo_rpe tum  gt.txt est.txt --delta 1 --delta_unit m -va             # drift per meter
evo_traj tum est1.txt est2.txt --ref gt.txt -p --plot_mode xz        # overlay trajectories
evo_res run*_ape.zip -p --save_table results.csv                     # compare result zips

The evo_traj plot tabs (xyz and rpy against time) are where per-axis drift becomes visible, and evo_res turns a directory of result zips into the comparison table and box plots for your report. Flags worth knowing: --align_origin (match only the first pose, useful when you want to see raw drift), --n_to_align (align on a subsequence), and --t_max_diff / --t_offset from the previous section. Exact flags move between versions, so trust evo_ape --help over any blog post, including this one.

For publication-grade odometry evaluation, rpg_trajectory_evaluation from the Zurich RPG group implements the methodology of Zhang and Scaramuzza's IROS 2018 tutorial: relative error computed over a spread of sub-trajectory lengths (say 10, 40, 90, 160, 250 m), presented as box plots of error against segment length, plus multi-run aggregation from a yaml config. That relative-error-versus-distance curve is the single most informative odometry plot in the field, because it shows how error grows, not just its average.

Half of real evaluation work is format wrangling, so learn the formats once:

TUM   : timestamp tx ty tz qx qy qz qw     one pose per line, seconds, gravity-free choice of frame
KITTI : r11 r12 r13 tx r21 r22 r23 ty r31 r32 r33 tz    3x4 row-major, NO timestamps (index-matched!)
EuRoC : csv, nanosecond timestamps, position then quaternion with w FIRST
Things that bite. KITTI format has no timestamps, so poses are matched by line index; drop a single frame in your estimator and every subsequent pose is compared against the wrong reference, producing garbage that looks like huge drift. EuRoC's w-first quaternion silently imported as x-first produces trajectories that are mirrored or tumbling; if your aligned plot looks like the reference's evil twin, check quaternion order before anything else. And a generous --t_max_diff will happily associate poses half a second apart without complaint.

Worked Setup A: Score a Trajectory with evo on TUM RGB-D. Goal: compute ATE and RPE for a real ORB-SLAM trajectory against motion-capture ground truth, read the statistics, and see what alignment choices do to them. Prereqs: Ubuntu 24.04, Python 3.12, ~200 MB disk. Fully self-contained: evo's repo ships an ORB-SLAM estimate and ground truth for the TUM fr2/desk sequence.

python3 -m venv ~/evo-env && source ~/evo-env/bin/activate

evo_traj tum fr2_desk_ORB.txt --ref fr2_desk_groundtruth.txt -pa --plot_mode xyz

evo_res ape.zip rpe.zip -p

What you should see: each command prints a statistics block (max, mean, median, min, rmse, sse, std). For this sequence expect an ATE rmse of a few centimeters; the plot window colors the trajectory by error, so you can see exactly where the estimator struggled (fast rotations over the desk, typically). The rmse-versus-median gap is your outlier detector: rmse well above median means a few bad segments, not uniform sloppiness.

Now break it on purpose. Re-run command 1 without -a: the number explodes to tens of centimeters or more, because you are now measuring the arbitrary offset between coordinate frames, not estimation error. Re-run with -as (Sim(3)): the number improves slightly, and that improvement is real scale error being absorbed by the alignment; legitimate for a monocular system, quiet self-deception for an RGB-D one. To go beyond the canned files, download the raw sequence from the TUM RGB-D download page (rgbd_dataset_freiburg2_desk.tgz contains groundtruth.txt) and run any system from Chapters 4 or 5 on it, saving its trajectory in TUM format.

Sanity checks: units are meters, so 0.5 rmse on a desk sequence means a broken pipeline, not a bad algorithm; if the aligned plot is mirrored, suspect quaternion convention; if only a handful of poses get associated, your timestamps are in the wrong epoch or unit.

8.4Ground Truth, and Where It Comes From

"Ground truth" is a marketing term. What datasets actually have is a reference measurement whose error is 10 to 100 times smaller than the thing being evaluated, and every source has a price, a working volume, and a failure mode you should be able to recite:

SourceAccuracyRateWhere it worksCostThe catch

The pseudo-ground-truth route deserves respect because it is the only one that scales to real environments. The recipe: map the space once with a survey-grade terrestrial scanner (a Leica BLK360 or similar, millimeter-accurate point cloud), tie it to surveyed control points, then register each sensor scan of your run into that prior map with ICP, at no real-time constraint. The Newer College dataset did exactly this to get centimeter-level reference poses for a full handheld trajectory, outdoors, where no mocap could follow. The Hilti challenge did the surveyed-control-points version on construction sites.

RTK (Real-Time Kinematic)
GNSS positioning using carrier-phase corrections from a nearby base station. A solid "fix" gives 1 to 2 cm; a "float" solution quietly degrades to decimeters, so log the fix status alongside the poses.
NTRIP
Corrections streamed over the internet from a network of reference stations, so you can have RTK without owning a base station. Coverage and latency vary by region and provider.
Control point
A surveyed marker with known coordinates. Ties your map, your reference scan, and the building drawings into one frame; the anchor of every pseudo-ground-truth pipeline.
Prism
The retroreflector a robotic total station locks onto and tracks. Gives millimeter positions of one point on the robot, so orientation must come from elsewhere or from multiple prisms.
Pseudo ground truth
A reference trajectory produced by a much better offline pipeline (survey map plus unconstrained ICP), blessed by human review and honest about its own 2 to 10 cm error.

Know the circularity trap in your bones. If your reference trajectory came from a lidar-ICP pipeline and your system under test is a lidar-ICP pipeline, shared systematic errors cancel and your scores flatter you. The reference must be independent in mechanism, not just in software version: survey instruments for a SLAM stack, or at minimum a far richer sensor suite run without real-time limits.

Ground truth is a measurement with much smaller error bars, never truth. Know the reference's own accuracy, and stop trusting comparisons the moment your system's errors approach it: a 1 cm SLAM cannot be ranked by a 2 cm RTK reference.

KITTI odometry is the automotive standard: 22 driving sequences from Karlsruhe, 11 with public ground truth and 11 held out for the leaderboard, scored by the segment-based drift metric of Section 8.2. Its 10 Hz cameras and abundant moving traffic make it a stress test for outlier rejection, and its long straight roads made monocular scale drift famous.

EuRoC MAV (Burri et al., IJRR 2016) is eleven drone flights, graded easy to difficult, with superb hardware synchronization. It has been the standard VIO table for a decade: every visual-inertial paper you read in Chapter 4 reports centimeter ATEs on MH_01 through V2_03. The difficult Vicon-room sequences still break systems with motion blur and near-darkness.

TUM RGB-D (Sturm et al., IROS 2012) is the dataset that taught the community how to evaluate at all: it shipped ATE, RPE, association scripts, and an online evaluation service alongside the data. Kinect v1 imagery, handheld and wheeled sequences, mocap reference. Still the default for RGB-D systems like Chapter 5's RTAB-Map.

TUM VI covers what EuRoC cannot: long handheld routes through corridors and outdoors where the mocap reference exists only in the room where runs start and end. It therefore measures accumulated drift honestly, and its photometrically calibrated fisheye stereo made it the benchmark of choice for direct methods.

Newer College and its successors matter because the reference covers the whole outdoor trajectory (the ICP-against-survey-scan trick above), so lidar SLAM can finally be graded away from a mocap cage. NCLT is the long-term-autonomy dataset: 27 sessions across 15 months of Michigan weather, the benchmark for whether your map survives seasons. The Hilti SLAM Challenge brought prize money and millimeter-surveyed construction sites, and promptly humbled a generation of systems that were excellent on EuRoC; its sequences are the closest public proxy to "real industrial deployment." M2DGR gives ground robots the multi-sensor treatment with the transitions that actually kill deployments (doorways to sunlight, elevators, night). And the DARPA SubT challenge was scored not by ATE but by reporting artifact positions within 5 m in underground courses, a reminder that in the end the customer metric is "did the map put the thing where the thing was."

Reading a results table now becomes pattern matching. A system that wins EuRoC but skips Hilti is telling you something. So is a lidar paper that reports KITTI (flat, structured, 64-beam) but not Newer College (handheld, sparse, vegetated). Absence of a dataset from a table is data.

8.6Scoring the Map Itself

Trajectory error is a proxy. If the deliverable is the map (Chapter 3's map products), you can and should score the map directly against a reference point cloud from a survey scanner. The two halves of that score pull against each other:

accuracy      = mean over p in map of      dist(p, reference)     "is what I built correct?"
completeness  = fraction of q in reference with dist(q, map) < tau  "did I build all of it?"
chamfer       = mean_{p in map} dist(p, ref)  +  mean_{q in ref} dist(q, map)
F-score(tau)  = harmonic mean of precision and recall at threshold tau

Accuracy alone rewards timid maps (only map what you are sure of); completeness alone rewards reckless ones (spray points everywhere). Chamfer distance and the F-score at a stated threshold (commonly 5 or 10 cm) combine both, and the reconstruction community brought the F-score convention over to neural mapping papers in Chapter 7. Use point-to-plane distances on structured scenes if you can; point-to-point penalizes sampling density differences that are not errors.

For 2D occupancy grids the comparison is cell-wise against a reference grid over the cells both maps claim to know: a confusion matrix of occupied/free, or simply whether a planner finds the same routes in both (a map metric your operations team actually cares about). When no reference exists at all, no-reference sharpness measures like mean map entropy and mean plane variance score how crisp the cloud is: compute the local surface distribution around each point and average its entropy; double walls and smeared ceilings show up as high entropy. They catch degradation between runs even though they cannot certify absolute accuracy.

Why do map metrics lag trajectory metrics in adoption? Reference clouds cost a survey scanner and a site day, alignment between map and reference adds its own ambiguity, and for decades the trajectory correlated well enough to stand in for the map. That logic inverts in industry: a surveying customer does not care where the scanner thought it was, only that the wall is where the wall is. Expect map-level evaluation to be the part of your protocol that impresses people.

8.7Benchmarking Your Own Robot Honestly

Public benchmarks rank algorithms. Your robot needs a different instrument: a repeatable protocol that detects regressions and predicts field behavior. A defensible baseline protocol looks like this:

  1. Fix the routes. Two or three routes marked with tape on the floor, driven the same direction at the same speed, including at least one full loop where the finish marker is the start marker. A loop gives you a free metric with zero equipment: return-to-start error, measured with a ruler against the tape.
  2. Run each route at least five times per configuration. One run is an anecdote. Report the median and the worst run, never the mean alone; SLAM error distributions are heavy-tailed and the mean hides that.
  3. Count hard failures separately. A run where tracking was lost, the map forked, or the robot needed rescue is not a large error to average in, it is a different variable. Report "median ATE 6 cm, worst 14 cm, 1 failure in 15 runs", never "mean 40 cm".
  4. Verify calibration the same day (Chapter 2's quick checks). Otherwise your A/B test of two algorithms is actually a test of Tuesday's extrinsics against Thursday's.
  5. Record everything to bags. Every evaluation run saved as raw sensor data means every future algorithm version can be scored on the exact same inputs, which converts your field time into a permanent private benchmark.

When a number regresses, resist the urge to tune. Ablate: change exactly one thing at a time (feature count, IMU weight, loop-closure threshold) and re-run the full protocol on the recorded bags, so the regression gets localized to a module instead of blamed on the weather. Ablation on recorded data costs CPU time; ablation by re-driving the robot costs days and adds noise.

Simulation earns a place here too, with its limits stated. Sim gives you perfect ground truth, infinite runs, and controllable hazards, which makes it ideal for logic and integration testing (does the loop closure fire, does the map merge). But rendered sensors flatter SLAM: perfect timestamps, no rolling shutter, clean lidar returns, textures with convenient gradients. Treat sim numbers as an upper bound on field performance and never let a sim-only improvement ship without a bag-replay confirmation.

Things that bite. Warm-up matters: the first run after power-on often scores worse (IMU bias not settled, exposure adapting), so either discard a warm-up lap by policy or randomize run order. People contaminate runs by walking through them, which is fine, that is your real environment, but log it so a bad number can be explained instead of deleted. And beware the demo route effect: a route chosen because it works is a route that stopped measuring anything months ago.
Honest robot benchmarking = fixed routes, five or more runs, median and worst reported, hard failures counted separately, same-day calibration, everything bagged. It fits on an index card and most teams still do not do it.

8.8The Seven Deadly Sins of SLAM Evaluation

Each of these appears in published papers and in internal slide decks. Learn to spot them in others' results and, less comfortably, in your own:

  1. The Single Golden Run. One trajectory, chosen after the fact, presented as typical. Nondeterministic systems (RANSAC, multi-threaded optimization) can vary 2x between identical runs; a single number is a coin flip you got to re-flip.
  2. Scale-Aligned Mono Presented as Metric. A Sim(3)-aligned monocular ATE in the same table as SE(3)-aligned stereo results, alignment group unstated. The mono number has been forgiven the one error class that dominates mono in practice.
  3. Grading on the Training Environment. Parameters tuned in the same building, same lighting, same route as the evaluation. The number measures memorization. Hold out an environment the way ML people hold out data.
  4. The Vanishing Failure Run. "Runs where tracking was lost were excluded." Excluded from the average, and from the paper, and from the reader's risk model. Failures are the most decision-relevant statistic you have.
  5. ATE-Only Reporting. A strong loop closure can pull global error down while local odometry jitters badly, and it is the jitter that shakes the controller and blurs the map. Without RPE alongside ATE, half the behavior is invisible.
  6. Tuning on the Test Set. Config files with per-sequence parameters for EuRoC sequences. If your yaml mentions MH_04 by name, the benchmark has stopped measuring generalization.
  7. Screenshot-Driven Development. Merging a change because the map "looks cleaner." Looks-cleaner does not survive contact with Section 8.1. Every one of the six sins above starts here, with the belief that eyes are a metric.

8.9Regression Testing SLAM in CI

Everything above becomes routine the day it runs on every commit. The pattern is golden-run replay: a small set of blessed recordings fed through the full SLAM stack in CI, the output trajectory scored with evo, and hard gates on the result. A SLAM regression then fails a pipeline instead of a customer site.

Three kinds of gates earn their keep. Accuracy gates: ATE rmse against the recording's reference trajectory below a threshold, with the threshold set from measured run-to-run variance (blessed median plus three standard deviations over ten replays), not from optimism. Resource gates: wall-clock processing time and peak memory as hard budgets, because a 20% CPU regression is a field failure on your smallest robot even when accuracy is untouched. Liveness gates: the stack produced poses for the whole recording, no gap longer than N seconds, loop closure fired on the loop route. Where does the reference trajectory come from? For a mocap or RTK-covered recording, from the instrument; for everything else, bless a pseudo-ground-truth run (Section 8.4): the full offline pipeline at leisure settings, reviewed once by a human, then frozen. The gate then measures drift from blessed behavior, which is regression testing, and only claims that.

Worked Setup B: a Minimal CI Regression Harness. Goal: a bash-plus-Python skeleton that runs your SLAM binary over a golden recording (a frozen dataset directory of scans or RGB-D frames), computes ATE with evo as a library, and exits nonzero if the gate fails. Any of this book's runnable systems fits the SLAM_CMD slot: kiss_icp_pipeline from Chapter 3, rtabmap-rgbd_dataset from Chapter 5, or your own C++ binary, as long as it writes a TUM-format trajectory. Prereqs: Ubuntu 24.04, pip install evo in the CI image.

#!/usr/bin/env python3
import sys
from evo.core import metrics, sync
from evo.tools import file_interface

ape.process_data((ref, est))
rmse = ape.get_statistic(metrics.StatisticsType.rmse)
print(f"ATE rmse {rmse:.4f} m  (gate {thresh} m, {est.num_poses} poses)")
sys.exit(0 if rmse < thresh else 1)

What you should see: on a healthy commit, a one-line pass like ATE rmse 0.0612 m (gate 0.15 m, 2841 poses); on a regression, a red pipeline with the number that broke it. Save the evo result zip as a CI artifact so the offending commit can be compared against the last good one with evo_res in one command.

Determinism notes, because replay is never perfectly repeatable: process the recording's own timestamps rather than the wall clock, so a slow CI runner does not change the data; run the stack single-threaded where the code allows, since thread scheduling is the main source of run-to-run variance; seed anything RANSAC-shaped; and pin the config file in the repo so a tuning change can never masquerade as a code regression. Then accept the residual variance honestly: measure the spread over ten replays once, set the gate with that margin, and optionally run three replays per commit gating on the median.

Sanity checks: if the gate passes with suspiciously few associated poses, the recorder started late or QoS dropped messages; if rmse is huge but the plot shape matches, frames or quaternion conventions differ between reference and estimate; if numbers drift over weeks with no code change, your CI runners are not CPU-isolated. Re-record or re-bless goldens only on a named event: sensor or driver change that alters message timing, calibration change, message definition migration, or an intended algorithm change whose new trajectory a human has reviewed, exactly like updating a snapshot test.

Golden-run replay plus an ATE gate plus resource budgets turns SLAM quality from a quarterly panic into a per-commit fact. The gate threshold comes from measured replay variance, and goldens change only through review, never by silent overwrite.

8.11Interview Questions

Explain ATE versus RPE, and when each one lies.

ATE aligns the estimated trajectory to ground truth with a best-fit rigid transform and measures per-pose residuals, so it scores global consistency. RPE compares motion increments over a fixed step, so it scores local drift and needs no alignment. ATE lies when loop closures mask terrible odometry: the global shape is fine while local motion is jittery, which wrecks control. RPE lies in the other direction: an odometry with slow systematic drift can post excellent per-second RPE while the map warps into uselessness over 500 meters. That is why serious reports include both, plus drift as a percentage of distance.

Why must you align trajectories before computing ATE, and what does Sim(3) alignment hide?

The estimator starts in an arbitrary frame, so the offset between estimate and reference frames is gauge freedom, not error; without alignment you mostly measure that arbitrary offset. Umeyama's closed-form least-squares fit removes it in SE(3). Sim(3) adds a free scale factor, which is mandatory for monocular systems where scale is unobservable, but it absorbs all global scale error. Applied to a stereo, RGB-D, or lidar result it silently deletes the error class those sensors are supposed to prevent, so the alignment group must always be stated next to the number.

Design an evaluation for a warehouse AMR with no motion capture.

Layer cheap references: taped fixed routes with return-to-start error measured by ruler on every loop; a handful of surveyed floor markers (total station or even careful tape measurement to building columns) that the robot must stop on, giving absolute checkpoints at centimeter level; and a pseudo-ground-truth trajectory from an offline run of the full pipeline against a survey-scanner map of the site if the budget allows one scanning day. Run five or more repetitions per route, report median and worst, count interventions separately, and bag everything so future versions replay the same data. The claim you can make honestly is checkpoint error and repeatability, not millimeter ATE, and saying so is part of the answer.

My system gets 2 cm ATE on EuRoC. Why might it still fail on my robot?

EuRoC is a gift: global-shutter cameras, an excellent synchronized IMU, a textured room, and short flights. Your robot has rolling shutter, worse time sync, vibration, blank walls, glass, sunlight transitions, and hours of runtime. There is also selection pressure: a decade of systems and their default parameters have been tuned against EuRoC, so scores partly measure benchmark familiarity. The number transfers only as far as the sensor quality, calibration discipline, and environment difficulty transfer, which is why datasets like Hilti exist and why field protocols with your own hardware are non-negotiable.

How many runs is enough, and what do you report?

Enough runs to see the spread, not just the center: five per configuration is a floor, ten if the system is nondeterministic, and the honest procedure is to keep adding runs until the median stabilizes. Report median and worst case, never mean alone, because SLAM errors are heavy-tailed and the mean is dominated by rare bad runs. Hard failures (lost tracking, forked map, rescue needed) are reported as a separate count, not averaged in as large errors, since a 2% failure rate is a different engineering problem than a 2 cm median.

Why does KITTI report drift as % per segment instead of a single ATE?

On a 4 km drive, a single ATE number is dominated by wherever the largest excursion happened and by how the alignment distributed it, so two systems with identical local quality can post wildly different ATEs. Averaging relative error over sub-trajectories of 100 to 800 m measures the drift rate itself, is comparable across routes of different lengths, and separates translation (%) from rotation (deg/m). It is RPE, industrialized. The cost is that it says nothing about global consistency, which for a mapping product you would still check separately.

How would you compare a monocular system against a stereo system fairly?

You cannot make one table pretend they solve the same problem. Align the mono result in Sim(3), the stereo in SE(3), label both, and additionally report the mono system's scale error over time (the ratio the Sim(3) fit chose, and how scale drifts within the run). If the application is metric, add an experiment where mono scale comes from its deployed source, IMU or known-height ground plane, and grade that combination in SE(3) like everything else. The senior answer names the asymmetry instead of hiding it in an alignment flag.

Your CI SLAM test gives different numbers on the same recording every run. Why, and what do you do?

Replay is not deterministic by default: thread scheduling reorders work, queues drop different messages under load, RANSAC seeds differ, and wall-clock timers fire at different data times. Reduce the variance first (drive processing from the recording's timestamps, run single-threaded where the code allows, seed the random components, pin the config), then measure what remains: run the replay ten times, take the spread, and set the CI gate at the blessed median plus a margin derived from that spread. Gating on the median of three runs per commit buys robustness cheaply. The wrong answer is loosening the threshold until the noise stops bothering you, because that also stops it from catching regressions.

How do you evaluate map quality when there is no reference cloud?

Fall back to internal-consistency and no-reference measures, stated as such. Mean map entropy and plane variance score crispness: double walls and smeared surfaces from bad trajectories raise local entropy, so the metric tracks degradation between versions. Loop consistency checks (map the same room from two directions, measure the disagreement), return-to-start error, and checkpoint markers give partial absolute anchors. For occupancy grids, planner-equivalence (does navigation find the same routes as on the blessed map) is a shippable criterion. None of these certify accuracy, and the honest phrasing is regression detection, not validation.
