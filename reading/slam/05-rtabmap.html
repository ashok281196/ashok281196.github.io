Chapter Five · The Budget Indoor Rig

RTAB-Map + RealSense D435i

For roughly the price of a mid-range phone you can build a robot that maps a building today and finds itself in that map tomorrow morning. This chapter is that rig, end to end.

5.1Why This Exact Pairing Matters

Bolt a RealSense D435i onto anything with wheels, launch RTAB-Map, and out come a 2D occupancy grid that Nav2 can consume directly, a colored 3D point cloud, and an OctoMap. The bill, at 2026 prices: the D435i lists at $334 and sells street for about $250 now that the D455 and D456 have taken over Intel's front page. Add a Raspberry Pi 5 (8 GB, about $80) or a Jetson Orin Nano Super (about $249) and the whole perception stack lands under $600. The software is free, apt-installable, and maintained by the same lab that wrote the papers.

That is why it is the standard indoor-UGV prototype rig, and why interviewers love it. Questions about this pairing test two things at once: whether you understand SLAM concepts, and whether you have actually fought real hardware. Plenty of candidates can define loop closure. Far fewer can explain why the map ghosts when the robot rotates, or why their depth image died when they swapped USB cables.

One piece of honesty up front. RTAB-Map does not win trajectory-accuracy benchmarks; ORB-SLAM3 beats it on ATE on most sequences (Chapter 7 covers how to measure that claim). RTAB-Map wins at something else: producing usable maps, staying up for hours, and surviving deployment. Those are different virtues, and for a product robot they are usually the ones that matter.

5.2RTAB-Map from Zero

RTAB-Map stands for Real-Time Appearance-Based Mapping, by Mathieu Labbé and François Michaud at the Université de Sherbrooke (ICRA 2011, IROS 2014, JFR 2019). Underneath, it is graph SLAM. Nodes hold RGB-D keyframes: a color image, a depth image, the visual words extracted from them, and the odometry pose where they were taken. Edges are constraints: odometry links between consecutive nodes, loop-closure links between recognized places, and proximity links between nodes that are near each other in space. Two ideas are its signature, and both are worth knowing cold.

Appearance-Based Loop Closure with a Bag of Words

"Appearance-based" means the robot recognizes places by how they look, not by matching geometry. The mechanism, step by step:

  1. Extract local visual features from the current keyframe. The default detector is GFTT corners with BRIEF descriptors; ORB is a common alternative, and SIFT/SURF are available if you built with the nonfree modules.
  2. Quantize each descriptor against a bag-of-visual-words vocabulary: a dictionary of typical feature appearances. The image becomes a histogram of which words it contains, the way you might describe a book by its word counts. Unlike ORB-SLAM's DBoW2, which ships a vocabulary pre-trained offline, RTAB-Map builds its vocabulary incrementally from the images it actually sees, adding new words when a descriptor matches nothing (nearest-neighbor search over a FLANN kd-tree).
  3. Score the current histogram against stored locations with a tf-idf-style likelihood: words that appear everywhere carry little weight, rare words carry a lot.

Historical context makes the name make sense. Around 2008-2012, appearance-based place recognition was the hot answer to loop closure at scale; FAB-MAP had shown you could recognize places over a 1000 km trajectory from images alone. RTAB-Map's contribution was making that style of recognition run online, forever, on a robot's own CPU, and wrapping a complete mapping system around it rather than leaving place recognition as a party trick.

The Bayesian Filter over Loop Hypotheses

A single good histogram match is not a loop closure. RTAB-Map runs a discrete Bayesian filter whose state is "which past location, if any, am I looking at right now?" It maintains a probability for every location in Working Memory plus one extra hypothesis: this is a new place. Each image updates the posterior in two steps you will recognize from Chapter 1:

predict:  belief spreads to each hypothesis' neighbors in the graph
          (if I was probably at node 42, I am now probably near node 42)
update:   multiply by the BoW likelihood of the current image
          against each location, then normalize

Evidence therefore has to accumulate over consecutive frames; one lucky match against a lookalike corridor gets diluted before it can do damage. When some location's posterior clears Rtabmap/LoopThr (default 0.11), the candidate goes to geometric verification: match individual features between the two keyframes, solve the relative camera pose with PnP + RANSAC, and accept only if at least Vis/MinInliers features agree (default 20). Only then is a loop-closure edge added and the graph re-optimized.

Visual word
A cluster of similar feature descriptors treated as one symbol. "Corner of a door frame, lit from the left" becomes word #4127.
tf-idf
Term frequency times inverse document frequency. A scoring trick from text retrieval: a word you see in every image tells you nothing about where you are.
PnP (Perspective-n-Point)
Given n known 3D points and their pixel locations in an image, solve for the camera pose. The workhorse of visual relocalization.
RANSAC
Fit a model to random small subsets of the data, keep the fit that the most points agree with. Standard armor against wrong feature matches.
Keyframe
A frame selected for long-term storage. Keeping all 30 fps forever is pointless; consecutive frames are nearly identical.

STM, WM, LTM: the Memory That Keeps the CPU Bounded

A naive system compares each new frame against every past location, so cost grows with map size until real time dies. RTAB-Map's fix is shaped like human memory, and it is the reason the system exists:

  • Short-Term Memory (STM). New nodes enter here first (Mem/STMSize, default 10 nodes). STM nodes are exempt from loop-closure detection, because you would trivially "recognize" the place you saw one second ago. STM is also where node weights are set: if consecutive images look alike, the robot is lingering, and the location's weight goes up.
  • Working Memory (WM). Nodes graduate here from STM. WM is the set actively searched by the Bayesian filter. This is the memory that costs CPU.
  • Long-Term Memory (LTM). When the map update exceeds the time budget Rtabmap/TimeThr (ms), or WM exceeds Rtabmap/MemoryThr (nodes), the oldest, lowest-weight locations are transferred out of WM into the SQLite database on disk. They are not deleted; they are just not searched.
  • Retrieval. When a loop closure lands next to a region that was transferred out, its neighbors are pulled back from LTM into WM. Return to an old wing of the building, recognize one doorway, and the whole wing wakes up.
new frame
   └─► STM (last ~10 nodes; no loop detection here)
          └─► WM (actively searched by the Bayesian filter)
                 ├─ update time > Rtabmap/TimeThr?  transfer oldest,
                 │  lowest-weight nodes ──► LTM (sqlite, on disk)
                 └─ loop closure near an LTM region? retrieve its
                    neighbors back ◄── LTM

The consequence: loop-closure cost is O(WM size), which is bounded by your own budget, not by the size of the environment. That is how RTAB-Map does multi-session, building-scale mapping in real time on a CPU, and it is the answer to "why does RTAB-Map scale?"

Working Memory is a barista's front-of-mind list of regulars: faces seen recently or often. Everyone else is in the back-room ledger (LTM). When a half-familiar face walks in and gets recognized, the barista also dusts off memories of that person's usual companions. Service time stays constant no matter how many customers the shop has ever had.
RTAB-Map bounds loop-closure cost by searching only a fixed-size Working Memory and shelving everything else in a database, retrieving neighborhoods on demand. Say "the WM/LTM memory management keeps detection under a fixed time budget" and you have given the one-sentence version of both papers.

Graph SLAM Underneath

Every accepted constraint feeds a pose graph: node poses as variables, odometry and loop-closure edges as measurements, solved by sparse nonlinear least squares exactly as in Chapter 1. RTAB-Map does not ship its own optimizer; it links one of TORO, g2o, GTSAM, or Ceres, selected by Optimizer/Strategy (0 through 3). The stock ROS binaries default to GTSAM. Optimization runs when a loop or proximity link is accepted, and the corrected poses propagate into every map product: the 2D grid, the cloud, and the OctoMap are all re-rendered from keyframes at their new poses, which is why the map visibly "snaps" straight when a big loop closes.

5.3The Full Architecture on a Robot

On ROS 2 the system is three or four nodes wired by topics, and most integration pain is wiring. The camera driver publishes color, aligned depth, and camera info. A synchronization node bundles them into a single rgbd_image message so the SLAM node never sees a color frame paired with the wrong depth frame. The rtabmap node consumes that plus an odometry stream.

Odometry, Three Honest Options

RTAB-Map is agnostic about where odometry comes from, and choosing is the first real engineering decision on this rig.

  • Wheel odometry. Smooth, cheap, always publishes, immune to lighting. Drifts with wheel slip, dies on carpet transitions and thresholds, and knows nothing about z. On a slow indoor base with decent encoders it is often the most robust choice, with the camera reserved for loop closure and mapping.
  • RTAB-Map's own RGB-D odometry (rgbd_odometry node). No extra hardware. The default registration is F2M, frame-to-local-map: track features against a small local map of recent keyframes rather than only the previous frame (F2F). F2M beats F2F for the same reason scan-to-map beats scan-to-scan in Chapter 3: the reference accumulates evidence instead of resetting every frame. Weaknesses: blank walls, darkness, fast rotation, and it eats a CPU core.
  • External VIO. VINS-Fusion, ORB-SLAM3 in odometry mode, or a vendor VIO module. Best behavior under fast or jerky motion because the IMU carries you through visual dropouts (Chapter 4 explains why). Costs integration effort and calibration care (Chapter 2).

Production robots frequently fuse wheel and visual odometry through a robot_localization EKF, so either source failing degrades accuracy instead of ending the run. RTAB-Map also adds proximity detection (RGBD/ProximityBySpace): it links nodes that odometry says are close in space even when appearance matching fails, which is what saves you when you re-traverse a corridor in the opposite direction and everything looks different.

What Comes Out

  • /map: a nav_msgs/OccupancyGrid, built from depth by the Grid/ parameters. This is what Nav2 eats.
  • /mapData and /mapGraph: the graph itself, nodes and edges, for visualization plugins and debugging.
  • /cloud_map: the assembled 3D point cloud, re-rendered from keyframes after each optimization.
  • OctoMap topics if enabled, for 3D collision checking (Chapter 3 discusses when you want them).
  • The transform map → odom, which is arguably the real product.

Where tf Sits, and Who Publishes map→odom

The frame contract from Chapter 0 applies verbatim:

map ──► odom ──► base_link ──► camera_link
 │        │           │
 │        │           └─ static, from your URDF
 │        └─ odometry source: fast, continuous, drifts
 └─ rtabmap: slow correction, absorbs loop-closure jumps

The odometry source publishes odom → base_link, fast and continuous but drifting. The rtabmap node publishes map → odom: a slow correction that absorbs loop-closure jumps, so the pose your controller consumes never teleports mid-motion. base_link → camera_link is a static transform from your URDF, and getting it wrong produces the ghost-map symptom in section 5.7. Chapter 8 builds Nav2 on exactly this contract.

One node owns each transform. Odometry owns odom→base_link and never jumps; rtabmap owns map→odom and absorbs every correction. If two things publish the same transform, or nothing publishes map→odom, navigation is broken before any parameter matters.

5.4The D435i, Hardware Facts to Get Exactly Right

Interviewers check these details because they separate people who read the box from people who shipped the camera.

  • Depth principle: active IR stereo. Two infrared imagers compute depth by stereo triangulation over a 50 mm baseline, and an IR dot projector sprays pseudo-random texture onto blank surfaces so the stereo matcher has something to grip. Because the underlying method is plain stereo, it degrades gracefully to passive stereo outdoors. It is not time-of-flight and not coded structured light (that was the older SR300 family); say the wrong one and the interview changes tone.
  • Shutters, the detail almost everyone gets wrong. The two depth/IR imagers are global shutter: the whole frame is captured at one instant, so fast motion does not warp geometry. The RGB sensor is rolling shutter: rows are read out sequentially, so fast yaw skews the color image. Consequence: feature tracking on RGB suffers exactly when the robot moves fast. The known trick is to run visual odometry on the left IR image instead, which is global shutter and shares the depth imager's viewpoint.
  • When to disable the emitter. The projector is rigidly attached to the camera, so its dots slide across surfaces as the camera moves. They are excellent texture for stereo matching (dots at one instant are consistent between the two IR imagers) but poison for feature tracking, because a dot is not a fixed 3D world point. If you run odometry on IR, either turn the emitter off, or use the driver's emitter on/off alternation so depth frames get dots and odometry frames get clean IR.
  • The "i" is a 6-DOF IMU, a Bosch BMI055. It is mediocre: fine for gravity direction and short-horizon motion prediction through a Madgwick or complementary filter, nowhere near good enough to be your only odometry. It ships uncalibrated; run rs-imu-calibration once per unit or your gravity vector is subtly wrong forever, and your camera-IMU extrinsics still need the Chapter 2 treatment if you feed it to a real VIO.
  • Envelope. Depth is trustworthy from about 0.3 m to 3 m. Error grows quadratically with distance, a stereo-baseline fact: depth error scales as z² divided by focal length times baseline. In practice that means a few millimeters at 1 m, roughly 2% at 2 m, and 4 to 6% by 4 m, at which point walls turn into fog. Depth FOV is about 87°×58°.
  • Knobs. Emitter power, visual presets (High Accuracy, High Density), and post-processing filters (decimation, spatial, temporal). Filters make clouds prettier and add latency, and temporal filtering smears moving objects into geometry that ICP will then trust. Nothing is free.

5.6The Database

Everything RTAB-Map knows lives in one file, by default ~/.ros/rtabmap.db, and it is a plain SQLite database. Open it with the sqlite3 CLI if you are curious; the schema has tables for nodes, links, visual words, and calibration. Each node stores its pose, its BoW signature, and compressed sensor data: JPEG for color, PNG for 16-bit depth. This single-file design is a quiet superpower: copying a map to another robot is scp, and a bug report can attach the entire session.

rtabmap-databaseViewer is the post-mortem tool and you should live in it. Open yesterday's database and you can scrub through every keyframe, see each loop closure with its inlier matches drawn between the two images, step through rejected closures and read why they failed (too few inliers, optimization error too high), inspect the graph before and after optimization, and regenerate 2D grids with different Grid/ parameters without re-driving the robot.

Multi-session mapping falls out of the memory design. Start rtabmap against an existing database without deleting it and a new session begins, initially disconnected from the old graph. The moment the Bayesian filter recognizes a place from a previous session, an inter-session loop closure welds the sessions together and optimization aligns them. Set Mem/InitWMWithAllNodes=true to preload the whole old map into WM at startup so that first weld happens sooner. This is how you map a building floor by floor across a week.

Size and pruning. Budget roughly half a megabyte per node at 848×480. At the default 1 Hz detection rate while moving, that is around 25 to 30 MB per minute; a 20-minute apartment session lands near 300 to 500 MB and a multi-hour facility run reaches several gigabytes. When that hurts: lower Rtabmap/DetectionRate, reduce image size, or set Mem/BinDataKept=false to stop storing raw images entirely (the map still works, but databaseViewer post-mortems and grid regeneration are gone, so leave it on during development). rtabmap-reprocess can rebuild a database with new parameters or assemble several databases into one.

ros2 launch realsense2_camera rs_launch.py \

    -r imu/data_raw:=/camera/camera/imu

ros2 launch rtabmap_launch rtabmap.launch.py \

    depth_topic:=/camera/camera/aligned_depth_to_color/image_raw \
    camera_info_topic:=/camera/camera/color/camera_info \
    frame_id:=camera_link approx_sync:=false \
    wait_imu_to_init:=true imu_topic:=/imu/data \
    rtabmap_args:="--delete_db_on_start \

      --Vis/MinInliers 25 --RGBD/OptimizeMaxError 3.0 \
      --Grid/RangeMax 3.0 --Grid/MaxObstacleHeight 1.5"

Now map like someone who knows how the sausage is made: move at walking pace, yaw slowly (under about 30°/s, rolling-shutter RGB and motion blur both punish fast turns), keep 1 to 2 m from surfaces, never stare at a blank wall, and deliberately close loops: end every excursion by returning through mapped territory to your start. Watch the rtabmap viewer; loop closures flash the matched frame pair.

When done, Ctrl-C rtabmap; the map is already saved at ~/.ros/rtabmap.db (the launch arg database_path moves it). Post-mortem:

rtabmap-databaseViewer ~/.ros/rtabmap.db

Open the constraints view and step through accepted and rejected loop closures; check the inlier counts against your Vis/MinInliers. Sanity checks: (1) ros2 topic hz /camera/camera/color/image_raw should show ~30 Hz, and if it is 6 Hz at low resolution, rs-enumerate-devices | grep -i usb will reveal a USB 2.1 downgrade, meaning cable or port. (2) "Did not receive data since 5 seconds" from rtabmap means topic names or QoS mismatch; re-check remaps before touching parameters. (3) Odometry flashing red in the viewer means visual odometry lost; slow down, add light, or fuse wheel odometry. (4) Double walls after a rotation mean extrinsics or time sync, not tuning; go back to Chapter 2.

5.7Field-Tested Gotchas

1. Featureless white walls kill visual odometry. No texture, no features, no motion estimate. Mitigations: run odometry on the IR image (the projector's dots are free texture for stereo, and with emitter alternation you still get clean frames for tracking), or fuse wheel odometry so a visual dropout is non-fatal.

2. Glass and mirrors lie in different directions. IR mostly passes through glass, so the camera maps the room behind the glass wall and the grid shows free space where a door will smash. Mirrors reflect the projector and paint a phantom room behind the wall, complete with a phantom robot. Neither is fixable in software alone; mark glass in the map by hand or add a sensor that sees it (sonar, or lidar at the right wavelength, Chapter 3).

3. Direct sunlight washes out the projector. Depth falls back to passive stereo: still works on textured surfaces, noisy on plain ones. South-facing rooms at 3 pm produce mysteriously patchy maps.

4. Repetitive texture invites false loop closures. Warehouse racking, rows of identical doors, tiled corridors. The Bayesian filter plus geometric verification stops most of them, but "most" is not "all". If a wrong closure gets in, the map folds onto itself. Raise Vis/MinInliers, keep RGBD/OptimizeMaxError strict, and inspect closures in databaseViewer before trusting a map from a repetitive site.

5. Dynamic scenes contaminate everything. People walking through frames leave smeared points in the cloud, occupy grid cells that are actually free, and donate features to odometry that make the world appear to move. Crowded-space mapping is its own discipline; at minimum, map off-hours and remove dynamic residue with databaseViewer grid regeneration.

6. The kidnapped robot. Power the robot down, carry it two rooms over, power it up. Odometry restarts at its origin and the graph now believes a lie. In localization mode RTAB-Map recovers at the first recognized view; in mapping mode you get a disconnected session that only welds on when a loop closure fires. Until then, do not trust the pose, and neither should your planner.

7. TF and time-sync bugs produce the classic ghost map. Wrong camera-to-robot extrinsics or unsynchronized clocks make the map double or smear when the robot rotates. Double walls after a turn: check transforms and timestamps before blaming the algorithm. Chapter 2 exists because of this failure mode.

8. USB 3 bandwidth is a hardware problem wearing a software costume. A marginal cable or an unpowered hub silently downgrades the link to USB 2, and the driver responds by cutting resolution and frame rate. Symptoms look like tuning problems. They are not. Use the stock cable, short, straight into a root port, and verify with rs-enumerate-devices.

9. Thermal throttling on SBCs. A Raspberry Pi 5 doing visual odometry plus BoW plus grid building runs hot; when it throttles, processing rate drops, sync windows are missed, and odometry quality collapses 20 minutes into a run that started beautifully. Heatsink, fan, and watch the clock speeds during a long session before declaring victory.

10. Multiple D435i in one room mostly coexist. Stereo tolerates alien dot patterns far better than coded light would, since dots are just texture, not a code. For clean multi-camera captures, stagger placement or use hardware sync.

11. The IMU ships uncalibrated. Skip rs-imu-calibration and gravity is slightly wrong on every run, forever, and you will chase the resulting tilt through every other parameter first.

5.8Localization-Only Mode, the Deployment Split

Set Mem/IncrementalMemory=false and RTAB-Map stops adding nodes. The same BoW machinery, the same Bayesian filter, the same geometric verification now serve one purpose: recognize where the robot is inside the stored graph and publish the map → odom correction. Nothing new is learned; CPU and memory flatten out; the database stops growing. This is the standard product lifecycle: map once, localize daily. Chapter 8 picks up the navigation half of that lifecycle.

Behavior on wake is worth stating precisely, because it drives system design. At startup the robot does not know where it is; until the first loop-closure-style match against the stored map, the map → odom transform is not corrected and any pose you read is a guess. Good deployments start the robot at a distinctive, well-mapped dock so relocalization fires within the first few frames. After that, localization updates arrive whenever a view matches the map, and odometry carries the robot between updates.

Then the world changes, because worlds do. A moved chair costs a few inliers and nothing else; the Bayesian filter over many words shrugs off partial change. Rearrange the furniture wholesale, change the lighting from daylight to sodium lamps, or repaint, and match scores sag until relocalization becomes sporadic exactly where the change happened. The honest options: remap on a schedule, or run occasional sessions in mapping mode (with Mem/InitWMWithAllNodes=true) so the map tracks the world, then return to localization mode. RTAB-Map's multi-session memory was built for exactly this maintenance loop.

Mapping mode and localization mode are the same algorithm with the learning switch flipped. Deployment means flipping Mem/IncrementalMemory to false, starting from a recognizable place, and having a plan for the day the world no longer matches the database.

Setup B: Relaunch Yesterday's Map in Localization Mode. Goal: boot the Setup A stack against the saved database, watch the robot relocalize in RViz, and hand Nav2 the exact tf and topic contract it expects. Prereqs: Setup A completed, its ~/.ros/rtabmap.db intact.

Camera and IMU filter launch exactly as in Setup A. Then rtabmap, two changes: localization:=true (which sets Mem/IncrementalMemory=false and Mem/InitWMWithAllNodes=true underneath) and no --delete_db_on_start:

ros2 launch rtabmap_launch rtabmap.launch.py \
    localization:=true rviz:=true \
    database_path:=~/.ros/rtabmap.db \
    rgb_topic:=/camera/camera/color/image_raw \
    depth_topic:=/camera/camera/aligned_depth_to_color/image_raw \
    camera_info_topic:=/camera/camera/color/camera_info \
    frame_id:=camera_link approx_sync:=false \
    wait_imu_to_init:=true imu_topic:=/imu/data

Verify relocalization: in RViz set the fixed frame to map, add a Map display on /map and a TF display. On startup the grid appears immediately (it is rendered from the database). Drive or carry the camera a few meters through mapped territory; the moment a view matches, the pose snaps to the right spot on the grid and tracks from there. Confirm the transform is alive:

ros2 run tf2_ros tf2_echo map odom

The contract Nav2 needs, and gets from this stack: a map frame with map → odom published by rtabmap; odom → base_link from your odometry source; and /map as a nav_msgs/OccupancyGrid built by the Grid/ parameters you chose during mapping. That triple (frame, tf, topic) is everything Chapter 8's costmaps and planners assume. Sanity checks: (1) if the pose never snaps, confirm you did not pass --delete_db_on_start out of habit; an empty database localizes against nothing. (2) If relocalization works near the dock but fails elsewhere, the world or the lighting changed; open databaseViewer and compare stored keyframes against what the camera sees now. (3) If /map is empty in RViz but the viewer shows a map, check QoS: the grid is latched (transient local), and a subscriber with volatile durability sees nothing.

5.9When to Outgrow This Rig

The rig has a ceiling, and recognizing it is cheaper than fighting it. Symptoms and their prescriptions:

  • Long corridors, big warehouses, outdoor yards. The D435i's useful depth ends near 3 m and sunlight eats the projector. You want lidar, and probably lidar-inertial odometry with it (Chapter 3). The safety conversation points the same way: a certified stop system wants a safety-rated lidar, which no RealSense will ever be.
  • Fast or aggressive motion. Drones, handheld scanning, anything that yaws faster than the tracker survives. You want tightly coupled VIO with a properly calibrated IMU, which is Chapter 4's territory, feeding RTAB-Map as the external odometry or replacing the stack outright.
  • Dense reconstruction or semantics as the product. If the deliverable is a mesh or a labeled scene rather than a navigation map, the learning-based systems of Chapter 6 and real GPU compute enter the picture.

What you keep even after outgrowing the camera: almost everything around it. RTAB-Map runs happily on lidar input (Reg/Strategy=1), the map-once-localize-daily lifecycle carries over unchanged, the database workflow and databaseViewer habits carry over, and the tf contract is identical no matter what sensor feeds it. The rig is a starter home; the plumbing is permanent.

5.11Interview Questions, Answer Out Loud First

Why does RTAB-Map stay real-time on multi-hour, building-scale runs?

Memory management. Loop-closure detection searches only the bounded Working Memory; when the update exceeds the time budget (Rtabmap/TimeThr) or the node budget (Rtabmap/MemoryThr), the oldest low-weight locations transfer to Long-Term Memory in the SQLite database, and they come back only when a loop closure lands near them. Search cost is O(WM size), a constant you chose, instead of O(map size). Graph optimization also stays local in practice. That bounded budget is the design point of the entire system, and it is why the same binary that maps a lab also maps a hospital.

BoW loop closure in a building with identical-looking rooms: what saves you from a false match?

Three layers. First, the Bayesian filter requires evidence to accumulate over consecutive frames, so one lucky histogram match gets diluted. Second, geometric verification: PnP with RANSAC must find a consistent relative pose with at least Vis/MinInliers agreeing features, and identical-looking rooms rarely agree in precise 3D layout. Third, RGBD/OptimizeMaxError checks the graph after optimization; a closure that makes the residuals explode gets rejected and removed. If all three fail, robust back-end kernels or switchable constraints can still down-weight the bad edge, but the honest answer is that you also inspect closures in databaseViewer before shipping a map from a repetitive site.

D435i vs a lidar for a warehouse robot?

Refuse the either/or. The camera is cheap and gives dense depth plus semantics (labels, barcodes, people), but has 3 m of useful range, a 87° FOV, and lighting sensitivity. A 2D lidar gives 360°, tens of meters, lighting immunity, and, decisive in a warehouse, can be part of a safety-rated stop system, which a RealSense cannot. The production answer is both: lidar for robust localization and certified safety, camera for perception and cheap 3D obstacle sensing. If forced to one sensor in a warehouse, it is the lidar, and Chapter 3 is why.

How do you get a 2D navigation map out of a 3D depth camera?

Take depth points within a height band that can actually hit the robot, roughly floor to robot height (Grid/MaxObstacleHeight), project them onto the ground plane as occupied cells, then raytrace from the camera through empty space to mark free cells, capped at Grid/RangeMax so far-field depth noise does not paint phantom walls. The result is exactly the nav_msgs/OccupancyGrid that Chapter 8's costmaps and planners consume. Bonus point: RTAB-Map re-renders this grid from keyframe poses after every optimization, so the grid straightens when loops close.

Visual odometry gets lost mid-run. What does the system do?

Layered recovery. Odom/ResetCountdown lets odometry reset itself after N lost frames instead of staying dead. Relocalization through the BoW machinery re-attaches the trajectory to the known map at the next recognized place. And best practice is fusing wheel odometry through a robot_localization EKF so a visual dropout degrades accuracy instead of ending the run. The theme to say out loud: single-sensor odometry is a single point of failure, so design for graceful degradation.

Wheel odometry or visual odometry under RTAB-Map, and why?

Depends on the floor and the walls, and a senior answer says so. Wheel odometry is smooth, cheap, and lighting-immune, but drifts with slip and is blind to z; on a slow base with good encoders it is often the more robust backbone, with the camera doing loop closure. RGB-D odometry needs no extra hardware and observes true motion, but dies on blank walls, in the dark, and under fast yaw, and it costs a CPU core. The best answer is fusion: EKF the wheels with the camera so each covers the other's failure mode, and let RTAB-Map's proximity detection patch the cases where appearance fails but position is known. Then name the failure you have seen: carpet transitions for wheels, white corridors for vision.

The map is fine, but the robot mislocalizes at the far corridor. How do you debug it?

Treat it as a data problem before a tuning problem. Open the database in rtabmap-databaseViewer and look at the far corridor's keyframes: are they feature-poor, motion-blurred, or visually identical to another corridor? Check rejected loop closures there and read the rejection reason, low inliers versus optimization error. Compare stored keyframes with what the camera sees now; if the corridor changed or the lighting did, the map is stale, not the localizer broken. If the frames are simply ambiguous, the fixes are physical and parametric: map that corridor with more viewpoints, add visual landmarks, raise Kp/MaxFeatures, and keep Vis/MinInliers strict so the system says lost rather than guessing wrong. A robot that knows it is lost is recoverable; a confidently wrong one is not.

Why would you disable the D435i's IR emitter, given that the dots help depth?

Because the projector is rigid to the camera, its dots slide across surfaces as the camera moves, so a dot is not a fixed 3D world point. For stereo matching within one instant the dots are pure profit; for feature tracking across time they are poison, since odometry locks onto them and sees motion that is not the world's. So when running visual odometry on the global-shutter IR images, disable the emitter or use emitter on/off alternation, which gives depth frames the dots and tracking frames a clean image. Explaining that distinction, matching within a frame versus tracking across frames, is the whole point of the question.

What actually changes when you set Mem/IncrementalMemory to false?

The learning stops and everything else stays. No new nodes or words are added, the database stops growing, and CPU flattens because Working Memory is fixed at the stored map. The BoW pipeline, Bayesian filter, and geometric verification now do relocalization: each match updates the map → odom transform instead of extending the graph. Two operational consequences follow. On wake the pose is unknown until the first match, so start the robot somewhere distinctive. And the map no longer tracks the world, so you need a maintenance plan, periodic remapping or occasional mapping-mode sessions, for the day the furniture wins.
