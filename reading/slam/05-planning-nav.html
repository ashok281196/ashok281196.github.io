Chapter Five · Closing the loop

Planning, navigation & localization on top of SLAM maps

A map nobody drives on is a screensaver. This chapter is what the robot does with everything the previous chapters built — starting with one contract that unlocks half the questions in this book.

5.1The frame contract — learn this first, it unlocks everything

map ──(localization: AMCL / SLAM loop closure)──► odom ──(wheel/VIO odometry)──► base_link

Chapter 0 introduced these frames; now the payoff. Two transforms, two personalities:

  • odom → base_link: published by odometry. Continuous, smooth, high-rate — and drifting. Never jumps.
  • map → odom: published by the localizer or SLAM system. It is the correction — and it jumps whenever a loop closure or relocalization fires.

Why split them: the local controller — the code deciding wheel velocities right now — consumes poses in the smooth odom frame, so the robot never teleports mid-control-loop. The global planner works in the map frame, where positions are actually correct. When loop closure snaps the map 30 cm, the global path replans, but the wheels never feel it.

"Loop closure jumped the map mid-navigation — what happens?" → "The map→odom correction jumps; odom→base_link stays smooth; the global planner replans in the map frame while the controller, isolated in the odom frame, never sees a discontinuity." This single answer, delivered calmly, wins the question from Chapter 1 and marks you as someone who has run a real robot.

SLAM built the map. In deployment you usually freeze it and just localize — cheaper, more stable, repeatable. The method depends on what kind of map you built:

The kidnapped robot problem

Power the robot on anywhere — no idea where it is. Global initialization options, one per modality: AMCL's global_localization scatters particles across the whole map and lets observations kill the wrong ones; Scan Context or BoW retrieval finds the best-matching stored place; outdoors, a GPS prior seeds the estimate.

Failure vocabulary worth owning: particle deprivation (resampling killed the particles near the truth — nobody left to be right) and symmetric environments (two identical corridors → the belief is genuinely multimodal, two blobs of probability). Particle filters can represent two blobs; a Kalman filter, being a single Gaussian, cannot — that is why Monte-Carlo methods own global localization.

5.3From map to plannable structure

  • 2D: occupancy grid → costmap. Inflate every obstacle by the robot's radius (so the planner can treat the robot as a point), then add a decaying cost skirt around obstacles ("legal but uncomfortable"). Nav2's costmap_2d stacks this in layers: static (the SLAM map) + obstacle/voxel (live sensors) + inflation.
  • 3D: project OctoMap down to 2.5D, or build the TSDF→ESDF distance field (Voxblox / nvblox / FIESTA — Chapter 1) so every point knows its distance-and-direction to the nearest obstacle; elevation maps for rough terrain.
The distinction that organizes all of planning: graph-search planners want discretized cost; optimization-based planners want distance fields (and their gradients).

5.4Global planners — the route

  • Dijkstra / A* on the costmap grid: expand cells outward from start, cheapest-first; A* adds a heuristic ("roughly how far to the goal?") to focus the search. Complete and optimal on the grid — the Nav2 default family. Theta* allows any-angle shortcuts.
  • Hybrid-A* (Dolgov, Thrun et al., DARPA Urban Challenge): A* whose states include heading and whose expansions are curves a car can actually drive (Dubins / Reeds-Shepp arcs) — for anything that can't turn in place. In Nav2: the Smac planner.
  • Sampling-based — RRT, RRT*, PRM (OMPL/MoveIt): grow a random tree through free space instead of gridding it. The answer for high-dimensional problems — a 7-joint arm lives in 7-D, where grids explode exponentially. RRT* is asymptotically optimal (path keeps improving with samples).
A* is checking every square of graph paper, cleverly ordered. RRT is throwing darts into the room and connecting the ones you can walk between. Graph paper wins in 2D; darts win in 7-D, because in 7-D there aren't enough atoms in the universe for graph paper.

5.5Local planners / controllers — the next two seconds

The global path is a suggestion; the local controller turns it into velocity commands while dodging what the map didn't know about:

  • DWA — Dynamic Window Approach (Fox, Burgard, Thrun, 1997): sample reachable (v, ω) velocity pairs, forward-simulate each for a second, score (progress, clearance, speed), pick the best. The legacy default.
  • TEB — Timed Elastic Band (Rösmann et al.): treat the local trajectory as an elastic band of poses with timestamps; optimize it under kinodynamic constraints. True trajectory optimization, fiddlier to tune.
  • MPPI — Model Predictive Path Integral (Williams et al., ICRA 2017): sample hundreds of control rollouts through your dynamics model, weight them by cost, blend into the command; repeat every cycle. Embarrassingly parallel (GPU-friendly), handles nonlinear dynamics, and is Nav2's modern default controller (2023+) — the current name to say.

Above the planners sits orchestration: the Nav2 BT Navigator runs a behavior tree coordinating planner, controller, smoother, and recovery behaviors (back up, spin, clear costmaps) when the robot gets stuck. Name-dropping the architecture — "BT orchestrating planner/controller/recovery servers" — signals you've seen the whole stack, not just one node.

Aerial sidebar: drones exploit differential flatness (quadrotor dynamics let you plan in position-space and recover full attitude/thrust) → smooth minimum-snap polynomial trajectories (Mellinger & Kumar, ICRA 2011); in clutter, EGO-Planner (2020) and Fast-Planner are the standard quadrotor local planners (ESDF-free vs ESDF-based).

5.6SLAM ↔ navigation coupling — the senior-level talking points

  1. Mapping while navigating: people walking by get baked into the static map as phantom walls. Separate the static layer (SLAM map) from a live obstacle layer with decay; lifelong mapping gates map updates on observation confidence.
  2. Loop closure during motion: handled — that's §5.1's contract.
  3. Dynamic environments: live sensor layers override the stale map; people-aware costmap layers add social margins around humans.
  4. No loop closures available (short drone mission, GPS-denied sprint): don't pretend — plan entirely in the odom frame and accept bounded drift. Common and correct on drones.
  5. Where goals come from: be able to trace the full pipeline of a real product — operator clicks a map in the UI → goal expressed in the map frame → Nav2 action → behavior tree → global plan → controller → velocity command over your middleware to the base. Tracing it end-to-end, on a system you've built, is a whole interview answer by itself.
  6. Safety layering: the planner is not the safety system. A certified reflex layer — lidar safety fields, E-stop — sits below the nav stack and can stop the robot no matter what the software above believes. The certification-friendly answer, and the correct one.
  7. Learning-based navigation (one sentence of currency): navigation foundation models (ViNT, NoMaD, 2023–24) and RL local planners are promising, but production in 2026 = classical stack + learned perception.

5.8Interview questions — answer out loud first

"A robot must navigate an office it mapped yesterday — walk me through the full stack."

Load the saved map → localize against it (AMCL on the grid, or RTAB-Map/hloc relocalization if visual) publishing map→odom → build layered costmaps (static + live obstacle + inflation) → global plan with A*/Smac in the map frame → MPPI local controller tracking it in the odom frame → BT Navigator orchestrating recoveries when stuck → and below everything, an independent safety reflex layer. Finish by stating the frame contract — smooth odom for control, jumpy-but-correct map for planning — and you've delivered a complete senior answer.

AMCL's particles all converge on the wrong corridor — why, and how do you fix it?

Why: the environment is symmetric — two corridors that look identical to the laser → the true posterior is multimodal, and aggressive resampling starved the mode that happened to be correct (particle deprivation). Fixes: more particles / gentler resampling to keep minority modes alive; inject random particles; trigger global relocalization; or bring in a sensor that breaks the symmetry — an appearance-based (camera) loop closure sees the posters on the wall that the lidar can't.

Why can't the local planner just work in the map frame directly?

Because the map-frame pose is allowed to jump whenever localization corrects itself. A controller mid-maneuver that sees its pose teleport 30 cm will command a violent correction for motion that never physically happened. The odom frame is guaranteed continuous — drifting, but smooth — which is exactly the property a feedback controller needs. Accuracy for the planner, continuity for the controller: two frames, one contract.

The grid A* path is jerky — what are your options?

Three tiers: (1) post-process — run a smoother (Nav2's smoother server) on the path; (2) plan better — a kinodynamic planner like Hybrid-A* that only generates drivable curves in the first place; (3) change representation — optimize the trajectory directly in an ESDF, where the distance gradient pulls the path smooth and clear of obstacles simultaneously. Bonus points for noting the controller (MPPI/TEB) also smooths execution regardless.

A person steps in front of the robot — which layer reacts, and at what latency?

Three timescales, know all three: the safety reflex layer (lidar safety field / certified stop) reacts in milliseconds, independent of all software above; the local controller (MPPI rollouts hitting the person's cost) reacts within a control cycle, tens of milliseconds, and steers around; the costmap obstacle layer → global replan reacts in hundreds of milliseconds if the path must change entirely. Safety is the layer you certify; the others are the layers you tune.

How would a drone's and a ground robot's nav stacks differ, on the same map?

Representation: UGV plans on a 2.5D costmap; the drone needs true 3D — an ESDF. Planner/controller: UGV = A*/Hybrid-A* + MPPI/TEB under wheel kinematics; drone = differential-flatness min-snap polynomials or EGO-Planner, tracked by a geometric controller. Localization sources differ too (wheel odom + lidar vs VIO + GPS/lidar). Same skeleton — localization, costmap/field, global, local, safety — different flesh at every layer; presenting it that way shows architectural thinking.
